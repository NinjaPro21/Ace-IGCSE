## Core idea

Ever wondered why the Sun seems to "rise" in the East or why we have shorter days in the winter? It isn't just about dates on a calendar; it is about the Earth's massive journey through space. Understanding how our planet spins and orbits is the foundation of navigation, timekeeping, and predicting the weather—skills essential for everything from sailing across oceans to planning global communication networks.

## Key definitions

- **Planet**: A large body, such as Earth, that orbits a star.
- **Star**: A massive ball of gas that produces energy by nuclear fusion (e.g., the Sun).
- **Natural Satellite**: A moon that orbits a planet as the planet orbits the Sun.
- **Rotation**: The spinning of the Earth on its axis, which takes approximately 24 hours to complete once.
- **Orbit**: The path a planet takes around a star, with Earth taking approximately 365 days to complete one revolution.
- **Axis**: An imaginary line through the North and South Poles, tilted at an angle of 23.5^\circrelative to the plane of the Earth's orbit.
- **Moon Phases**: The changing appearance of the Moon as seen from Earth, caused by its 28-day orbit around our planet.

## Steps / method

Exam Method for Orbital Speed Calculations:

Unit Conversion: Convert the orbital radius ($r$) from kilometres to metres (e.g., $1\ km = 1000\ m$).

Time Conversion: Convert the orbital period ($T$) into seconds (e.g., $days \times 24 \times 60 \times 60$).

Substitution: Plug the values into the formula $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$.

Final Calculation: Ensure the final answer is in m/s unless the question specifies otherwise.

## Common mistakes

Confusion of Rotation vs. Orbit: Thinking the Sun moves around the Earth; the Sun's daily motion is only apparent due to Earth's rotation.

Distance vs. Tilt: Believing summer happens because Earth is closer to the Sun; seasons are caused strictly by the tilt of the axis.

Unit Errors: Forgetting to convert the orbital period into seconds, leading to speeds that are far too low.

## Examiner tip

Nuclear Fusion: If asked how the Sun produces energy, you must state it is via nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium.

Moon Visibility: Always mention that the Moon is non-luminous and is visible only because it reflects light from the Sun.

Precision: When defining a year, use "365 days" but be aware that the actual orbit is roughly 365 days and 6 hours (hence leap years).

## Worked example — Identifying Moon Phases

Question: Seven days after a Full Moon is observed, what will the Moon look like, and what is this phase called?

Analyze the Cycle: The Moon's full orbit takes approximately 28 days.

Calculate the Fraction: 7 days is exactly $1/4$ of the 28-day cycle.

Identify Appearance: After a Full Moon, the illuminated portion begins to decrease (waning).

Conclusion: The Moon will be half-illuminated; this is the Third Quarter phase. ### Common Trap
