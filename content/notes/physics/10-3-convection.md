## Core idea

Convection is the primary method of thermal energy transfer in fluids (liquids and gases). Unlike conduction, which involves energy passing between particles in fixed positions, convection involves the bulk movement of the heated material itself due to changes in density.

## Key definitions

- **Convection**: The transfer of thermal energy in a fluid by means of convection currents caused by a difference in density.
- **Convection Current**: A circulating stream of fluid caused by the rising of warmer, less dense areas and the sinking of cooler, denser areas.
- **Fluid**: Any substance that has the ability to flow, specifically liquids and gases, because their particles are free to move.
Mechanism of Convection
- **Convection is driven by the relationship between temperature and density**: - **Heating and Expansion**: When a fluid is heated, its particles gain kinetic energy and move further apart.
- **Density Decrease**: Because there is now more space between the molecules, the heated region becomes less dense than the surrounding cooler fluid.
- **Rising and Sinking**: The warmer, less dense fluid rises. Cooler, denser fluid moves in to take its place and is subsequently heated, while the rising fluid cools at the top, becomes denser, and sinks.
- **Circulation**: This continuous cycle of rising and sinking creates a circulating convection current that distributes thermal energy throughout the fluid.
Demonstrating Convection
In Liquids (The Potassium Permanganate Experiment)
- **Setup**: Place a crystal of purple potassium permanganate at the bottom of a flask filled with water and heat the base directly under the crystal.
- **Observation**: A purple stream of dissolved crystal rises to the top, fans out, and then sinks down the sides of the flask.
- **Conclusion**: The purple stream traces the path of the convection current as the heated water at the bottom rises and the cooler water at the top sinks.
In Gases (The Smoke Box Experiment)
- **Setup**: A box with two chimneys has a lit candle under one chimney and a smouldering incense stick held over the other.
- **Observation**: Smoke from the incense stick is drawn down the first chimney, across the box, and then rises out of the chimney above the candle.
- **Conclusion**: The air above the candle is heated, expands, and rises. Cooler, denser air from outside sinks down the other chimney to replace it, carrying the smoke along to show the current.
Convection vs. Conduction
- **Solids**: Convection cannot occur in solids because their particles are in fixed positions and cannot move in bulk to carry thermal energy.
- **Liquids**: While liquids can conduct heat, they are poor thermal conductors. To prove this, water can be heated at the top of a tube; the water at the top will boil while an ice cube at the bottom (prevented from rising by a weight) remains frozen. This shows that without convection (which only happens when heated from the bottom), thermal energy transfer through the liquid is extremely slow.
