## Core idea

Convection is the transfer of thermal energy in fluids (liquids and gases) by the bulk movement of the heated fluid itself. Warm fluid expands, becomes less dense, rises, and is replaced by cooler, denser fluid — creating a convection current.

## Key definitions

- **Convection**: Transfer of thermal energy in a fluid by convection currents caused by density differences.
- **Convection current**: A circulating stream of fluid — warm fluid rises, cools, sinks, and is reheated.
- **Fluid**: Any substance that can flow (liquids and gases).

## Mechanism of convection

1. **Heating**: Fluid near the heat source gains kinetic energy and expands.
2. **Density decrease**: The heated region becomes less dense than surrounding cooler fluid.
3. **Rising**: Warm, less dense fluid rises.
4. **Cooling and sinking**: Fluid cools at the top, becomes denser, and sinks.
5. **Circulation**: This cycle creates a convection current that distributes thermal energy.

**Why convection cannot occur in solids**: Particles in solids are in fixed positions and cannot move in bulk to carry energy.

## Demonstrating convection

**In liquids (potassium permanganate experiment)**

1. Place a crystal of potassium permanganate at the bottom of a flask of water.
2. Heat the base of the flask directly under the crystal.
3. Observe: a purple stream rises, fans out at the top, and sinks down the sides.
4. Conclusion: the purple stream traces the convection current.

**In gases (smoke box experiment)**

1. A box with two chimneys has a lit candle under one chimney.
2. Hold a smouldering incense stick over the other chimney.
3. Observe: smoke is drawn down one chimney, across the box, and rises out above the candle.
4. Conclusion: heated air rises above the candle; cooler air sinks down the other chimney, carrying smoke with it.

## Steps / method

**Explaining convection (exam method):**

1. State that the fluid is heated and particles gain kinetic energy.
2. Explain that the fluid expands and becomes less dense.
3. Describe warm fluid rising and cooler fluid sinking to replace it.
4. Conclude that a convection current transfers thermal energy throughout the fluid.

## Common mistakes

**Convection in solids**: Convection requires bulk movement of fluid — it cannot happen in solids.

**Heating from the top**: If a liquid is heated at the top, convection does not circulate effectively (hot fluid stays at the top).

**Confusing with conduction**: In fluids, convection is usually the dominant method; conduction alone is very slow in liquids.

## Examiner tip

Link **density** to **temperature** explicitly: "warm air is less dense, so it rises." For sea breeze questions, compare how quickly land and sea heat up and cool down.

## Worked example — Sea Breeze

Question: Explain why a cool breeze blows from the sea to the land on a hot summer afternoon.

1. **Day time heating**: Land heats up faster than the sea.

2. **Air above land**: Warm air above the land expands, becomes less dense, and rises.

3. **Replacement air**: Cooler, denser air from above the sea moves inland to replace the rising warm air.

4. **Conclusion**: This convection current is felt as a sea breeze.

**Final answer:** Land heats faster than the sea; warm air rises over land and cooler air from the sea moves in to replace it (convection).
