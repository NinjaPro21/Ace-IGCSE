## Core idea

How do you get a heavy trolley moving, and why does it eventually stop? From rockets launching satellites into space to the simple act of walking across a room, the relationship between forces and motion determines how everything in our universe behaves. Mastering this topic allows you to calculate exactly how much push is needed to change an object's speed or direction and explains why you feel a pull toward the centre when a car turns a corner.

## Key definitions

- **Resultant Force**: A single force that has the same effect as all the forces acting on an object combined. (SI unit: newton, N).
- **Mass**: A measure of the quantity of matter in an object at rest relative to the observer. (SI unit: kilogram, kg).
- **Acceleration**: The change of velocity per unit time. (SI unit: $m/s^2$).
- **Friction**: A force that impedes motion and acts in the opposite direction to the motion of an object. (SI unit: newton, N).
- **Drag**: Frictional force acting on an object moving through a liquid or a gas.

## Key formulas

**Resultant force (Newton's second law)**

$F = ma$

Applies to objects with constant mass to calculate the acceleration produced by an unbalanced force.

**Linear resultant force**

$F_{\text{net}} = F_1 + F_2 + \cdots$

Used to determine the net force acting along a straight line; assign one direction as positive and the opposite as negative.

**Circular motion resultant force**

$F \propto \frac{mv^2}{r}$

The centripetal force increases with mass and speed, and decreases with radius.

**Velocity-Time Graphs**

- **Constant speed**: A horizontal line; indicates zero resultant force.
- **Uniform acceleration**: A straight line sloping upwards; indicates a constant resultant force.
- **Uniform deceleration**: A straight line sloping downwards; indicates a constant opposing force like friction.
- **Changing acceleration**: A curved line where the gradient changes over time.
- **Interpretations**: The gradient of a distance-time graph gives speed; the gradient of a speed-time graph gives acceleration; the area under a speed-time graph gives distance travelled.

## Steps / method

Method for solving resultant force and motion problems:

Identify the scalars and vectors: Mass is a scalar (magnitude only), while force, velocity, and acceleration are vectors (magnitude and direction).

Assign a positive direction: For forces acting in a straight line, choose one direction (e.g., forward or right) to be positive (+). Calculate the resultant force ($F$): Sum all forces acting on the object.

Determine the state of motion: If $F = 0$ (balanced), the object stays at rest or continues at a constant speed in a straight line. If $F \neq 0$ (unbalanced), the object will accelerate in the direction of the resultant force. Apply $F = ma$: Substitute the resultant force and mass into the formula to find acceleration.

## Common mistakes

Ignoring Direction: Adding forces like $10 \text{ N}$ and $10 \text{ N}$ to get $10 \text{ N}$ without realizing they are in opposite directions (which would result in $0 \text{ N}$).

Friction Direction: Forgetting that friction always acts in the opposite direction to motion, never in the same direction.

Zero Force Misconception: Believing an object must stop if the resultant force is zero; in reality, it continues at a constant speed in a straight line.

## Examiner tip

Circular Motion: When describing an object in a circular path, always state that the resultant force acts perpendicular to the motion and points towards the centre of the circle.

Newton's definition: Remember that 1 N is the force required to accelerate a 1 kg mass at $1\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Show Units: Ensure acceleration is in $m/s^2$ and forces are in N to avoid losing marks.

## Worked example — Finding Resultant Force

Question: A ball is pushed forward with 12 N and experiences air resistance of 8 N. Calculate the resultant force. Let the forward direction be positive (+).

1. Forward force $= +12\ \text{N}$; air resistance $= -8\ \text{N}$.

2. Resultant force $= +12 + (-8) = +4\ \text{N}$.

**Final answer:** $4\ \text{N}$ in the forward direction.

## Worked example — Acceleration from rest

Question: A 20 kg object at rest is acted upon by a 50 N force to the right and a 10 N friction force to the left. Calculate its velocity after 2 s.

1. **Resultant force:** $F = 50 - 10 = 40\ \text{N}$ to the right.

2. **Acceleration:** $a = F/m = 40/20 = 2\ \text{m/s}^2$.

3. **Final velocity:** $v = u + at = 0 + 2 \times 2 = 4\ \text{m/s}$ to the right.

**Final answer:** $4\ \text{m/s}$ to the right.

### Common Trap

Students often use the applied force (50 N) instead of the resultant force (40 N) in the $F = ma$ calculation.
