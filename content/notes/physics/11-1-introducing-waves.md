## Core idea

Waves are all around us, from the ripples on a pond to the invisible signals reaching your mobile phone. At its core, a wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one location to another. Crucially, while energy moves through the material, the material itself (the medium) does not travel with the wave. Understanding these properties is the first step toward mastering the physics of light, sound, and the electromagnetic spectrum.

## Key definitions

- **Wave motion**: Periodic motion that repeats itself at regular intervals.
- **Oscillation / vibration**: One complete cycle of periodic motion; the fundamental source of any wave.
- **Wave**: A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter.
- **Medium**: The substance through which a wave travels (e.g. rope, water, air).
- **Propagate**: How a wave spreads or travels through a medium.
- **Transverse wave**: The vibration is perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction of wave motion.
- **Longitudinal wave**: The vibration is parallel to the direction of wave motion.

## Energy vs. matter transfer

A common misconception is that a wave carries the medium along with it. Waves transfer **energy only**, not matter.

- **Evidence**: A bottle floating on a pond bobs up and down when ripples pass but is not carried away — water particles move locally while energy travels outward.
- **Work**: Because waves carry energy, they can do work (e.g. moving your body in the sea, generating electricity from wave power).

## Types of waves

**Transverse waves**

- Particles move at right angles to the direction the wave travels.
- Look like "humps and hollows" (crests and troughs).
- Examples: water waves, rope waves, electromagnetic waves (including light), seismic S-waves.

**Longitudinal waves**

- Particles vibrate back and forth in the same direction as the wave.
- Appear as compressions (particles close together) and rarefactions (particles spread apart).
- Examples: sound waves, seismic P-waves.

## Steps / method

Method for demonstrating wave types with a Slinky spring:

1. **Fixed end**: Secure one end of the Slinky to a wall or have a partner hold it.
2. **Transverse waves**: Move your hand rapidly left–right (or up–down) at right angles to the spring. You will see humps move toward the fixed end while coils only move sideways.
3. **Longitudinal waves**: Push and pull the free end rapidly toward and away from the fixed end. You will see compressed bands travel along the spring while coils oscillate back and forth.

## Common mistakes

- **Matter movement**: Thinking air molecules travel from a speaker to your ear. They only vibrate in place; the energy of the vibration travels to you.
- **Transverse vs. longitudinal**: Remember — transverse is at a T-junction (right angles); longitudinal is linear (parallel).

## Examiner tip

- When defining a wave, always state that it transfers **energy without transferring matter**.
- Light is always transverse; sound is always longitudinal.
