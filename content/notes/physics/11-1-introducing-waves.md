## Core idea

Waves are all around us, from the ripples on a pond to the invisible signals reaching your mobile phone. At its core, a wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one location to another. Crucially, while energy moves through the material, the material itself (the medium) does not travel with the wave. Understanding these properties is the first step toward mastering the physics of light, sound, and the electromagnetic spectrum.

## Key definitions

- **Wave Motion**: Periodic motion, or motion that repeats itself at regular intervals.
- **Oscillation / Vibration**: One complete cycle of periodic motion; it is the fundamental source of any wave.
- **Wave**: A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter.
- **Medium**: The substance or matter through which a wave travels, such as a rope, water, or air.
- **Propagate**: A term used to describe how a wave spreads, moves, or travels through a medium.
- **Transverse Waves**: Waves where the direction of vibration is perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction of wave motion.
- **Longitudinal Waves**: Waves where the direction of vibration is parallel to the direction of wave motion.
Energy vs. Matter Transfer
A common misconception is that a wave carries the medium along with it. However, waves transfer energy only.
- **Evidence**: If you place an empty bottle on a pond and create ripples, the bottle will bob up and down (vibrate) but will not be carried away by the ripples. This demonstrates that the water particles are only moving locally while the energy travels outward.
- **Work**: Because waves carry energy, they can do work, such as moving your body when you are hit by a large wave at sea or generating electricity through wave power.
Types of Waves
Transverse Waves
In these waves, the particles move up and down (or left and right) while the wave travels forward.
- **Visuals**: They look like "humps and hollows" (crests and troughs).
- **Examples**: Water waves, rope waves, electromagnetic waves (including light), and seismic S-waves (secondary waves).
Longitudinal Waves
In these waves, the particles vibrate back and forth in the same direction that the wave is moving.
- **Visuals**: They appear as a series of compressions (where particles are squashed together) and rarefactions (where particles are stretched apart).
- **Examples**: Sound waves and seismic P-waves (primary waves).

## Steps / method

Method for demonstrating wave types with a Slinky spring:

Fixed End: Secure one end of the Slinky to a wall or have a partner hold it.

To Create Transverse Waves: Move your hand rapidly from left to right (or up and down) at right angles to the spring. You will see "humps" move toward the fixed end while the individual coils only move sideways.

To Create Longitudinal Waves: Push and pull the free end of the Slinky rapidly toward and away from the fixed end. You will see dark bands of compressed coils travelling along the spring while the coils themselves only oscillate back and forth.

## Common mistakes

Matter Movement: Thinking that air molecules move from a speaker to your ear when you hear a sound. In reality, the air molecules only vibrate in place; the energy of the vibration is what travels to you.

Transverse vs. Longitudinal: Confusing the two. Remember: Transverse is at a T-junction (right angles), and Longitudinal is Linear (parallel).

## Examiner tip

Terminology: When asked to define a wave, always specify that it transfers energy without transferring matter to secure full marks.

Standard Examples: Be ready to categorize specific waves. Light is always transverse; sound is always longitudinal.
