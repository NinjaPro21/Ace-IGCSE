## Core idea

Energy Resources and Efficiency

Every time you charge your phone, eat a meal, or turn on a light, you are using energy harvested from our planet. Understanding where this energy comes from—and how much of it we waste—is key to building a sustainable future. In Physics, we categorize these sources and use mathematics to determine how effectively our machines convert one form of energy into another.

## Key definitions

- **Renewable Energy Resource**: An energy source that can be continually regenerated, such as wind or hydropower.
- **Non-renewable Energy Resource**: An energy source that is limited and will eventually run out, such as fossil fuels or nuclear fuel.
- **Efficiency**: The ratio of useful energy (or power) output to the total energy (or power) input, usually expressed as a percentage.
- **Nuclear Fusion**: The process where light atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous amounts of energy; this is the process that powers the Sun.
- **Nuclear Fission**: The process where a heavy atomic nucleus (like uranium) splits into two parts, releasing a huge amount of energy used in power stations.

## Key formulas

**Efficiency (Energy)**

$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{total energy input}} \times 100\%$

Used to find how much input energy is converted into a desired form.

**Efficiency (Power)**

$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful power output}}{\text{total power input}} \times 100\%$

Applies when using power ratings (Watts) instead of total energy (Joules).

**Total Energy Balance**

$\text{Total energy input} = \text{useful energy output} + \text{wasted energy output}$

Applies to any machine or energy transfer based on the principle of conservation of energy.

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Sankey Diagrams**: A visual summary used to represent energy conversions involving multiple stages.
- **Input**: The total energy enters from the left.
- **Useful Output**: The branch pointing to the right.
- **Wasted Energy**: The branch pointing downwards, representing dissipated energy like heat and sound.
- **Interpretation**: The thickness of each branch is proportional to the amount of energy it represents.
- **Energy Flow Diagrams**: A sequence of arrows showing the transition between energy stores (e.g., Chemical $\rightarrow$ Kinetic $\rightarrow$ GPE).

## Steps / method

Method for calculating efficiency and wasted energy:

Identify Total Input: Determine the total energy or power supplied to the system.

Identify Useful Output: Determine which part of the energy is doing the intended work.

Check Units: Ensure both input and output are in the same units (e.g., both in Joules or both in Watts).

Calculate Efficiency: Divide useful output by total input and multiply by 100.

Calculate Wasted Energy: Subtract the useful output from the total input to find how much energy was dissipated as heat or sound.

## Common mistakes

Energy "used up": Scientifically, energy is never used up or destroyed; it is merely dissipated or spread out into the surroundings.

Efficiency over 100%: In the real world, the useful energy output is always less than the energy input due to friction and thermal dissipation.

Ignoring the Sun: Forgetting that almost all our energy sources (wind, solar, fossil fuels, hydropower) are traced back to the Sun.

## Examiner tip

The Sun's Exceptions: You must know that geothermal, nuclear, and tidal energy are the only resources that do not have the Sun as their primary source.

Units: Efficiency is a percentage and does not have units like N or J.

Significant Figures: Provide your final percentage to 2 or 3 significant figures unless the question specifies otherwise.

## Worked example — Basic Efficiency

Question: A machine produces 35 J of useful output energy for every 50 J of total energy input. Calculate the efficiency of the machine.

Identify useful output = 35 J.

Identify total input = 50 J.

Use the formula $\text{Efficiency} = (35 / 50) \times 100\%.70\%$.

## Worked example — Wasted Energy in a Power Station
