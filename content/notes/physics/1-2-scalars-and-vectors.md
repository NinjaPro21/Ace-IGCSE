## Core idea

In physics, knowing "how much" is often only half the story. If a pilot is told to fly at 500 km/h but not in which direction, they will never reach their destination. Understanding the difference between scalars and vectors is vital for navigating the world, calculating the combined impact of forces on a bridge, or predicting the path of a moving object.

## Key definitions

- **Scalar Quantity**: A physical quantity that has magnitude (size) only. Examples include distance (m), speed (m/s), and mass (kg).
- **Vector Quantity**: A physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Examples include displacement (m), velocity (m/s), force (N), and acceleration ($m/s^2$).
- **Resultant Vector**: A single vector that produces the same effect as two or more vectors combined.
- **SI Unit for Force**: Newton (N).

## Key formulas

**Resultant of two vectors at right angles ($90^\circ$)**

$R = \sqrt{F_1^2 + F_2^2}$

Applies only when vectors are perpendicular to each other 2

**Direction of a resultant vector**

$\tan \theta = \frac{F_2}{F_1}$

Used to find the angle $\theta$ of the resultant force relative to a horizontal or vertical baseline.

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Vector Representation**: A vector is represented by an arrow. The length of the arrow is proportional to the magnitude, and the arrow points in the vector's direction.
- **Resultant Indication**: A resultant vector is usually indicated in diagrams by a double-headed arrow.
- **Motion Graph Interpretation**: Distance-time graph: The gradient gives the speed (a scalar). A straight line shows constant speed; a curve shows acceleration or deceleration.
- **Velocity-time graph**: The gradient gives the acceleration (a vector). The area under the graph gives the displacement (a vector).

## Steps / method

Method for finding the resultant of two vectors at right angles:

Choose a Method: Decide between the graphical parallelogram method or the calculation method.

Graphical Method: Choose an appropriate scale (e.g., 1 cm : 5 N). Draw a horizontal baseline and measure the required angle with a protractor. Draw the two vectors as sides of a parallelogram. Draw the diagonal from the origin; its length represents the resultant magnitude, and its angle represents the direction.

Calculation Method: Use Pythagoras’ Theorem to find the magnitude of the resultant. Use the tangent function ($\tan \theta$) to find the direction (angle).

## Common mistakes

Ignoring Direction: Adding vectors like $1 \text{ N}$ and $1 \text{ N}$ to get $1 \text{ N}$ without checking if they point in the same direction$.

Scale Errors: In graphical diagrams, failing to use a consistent scale or forgetting to convert the measured length back into the actual unit (e.g., N or m/s).

Incorrect Baseline: Measuring the angle from the vertical when the question asks for the angle from the horizontal.

## Examiner tip

Significant Figures: Ensure calculation results (like $36.9^\circ$) are rounded to a sensible number of significant figures (usually 2 or 3).

Show Your Scale: When drawing vector diagrams, always clearly state the scale used (e.g., "Scale 1 cm : 2 N").

Arrowheads: Always include arrowheads on vector lines to indicate direction; use double arrowheads specifically for resultants.

## Worked example — Adding Parallel Forces

Question: Two forces, 3 N and 5 N, act on a block in the same direction (towards the right). Calculate the resultant force. Assign a positive direction. Let "right" be positive (+).

1. Sum the magnitudes: $+3\ \text{N} + 5\ \text{N} = +8\ \text{N}$.

2. **Final answer:** The resultant force is $8\ \text{N}$ directed towards the right.

## Worked example — Vectors at Right Angles

Question: A force $F_1$ of $4\ \text{N}$ acts horizontally to the right. A second force $F_2$ of $3\ \text{N}$ acts vertically upwards. Determine the magnitude and direction of the resultant force $R$.

1. Use Pythagoras for magnitude: $R = \sqrt{4^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{16 + 9} = \sqrt{25} = 5\ \text{N}$.

2. **Final answer:** The resultant force is $5\ \text{N}$ at an angle of $36.9^\circ$ above the horizontal.

### Common Trap

Students often confuse distance (scalar) with displacement (vector). If you walk 10 m East and 10 m West, your total distance is 20 m, but your displacement is 0 m.
