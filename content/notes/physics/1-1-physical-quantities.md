## Core idea

Physics is the study of our natural world, from the vast solar system to the tiny atom. To understand how matter and energy interact, scientists conduct experiments that require accurate measurements to obtain reliable results. Whether monitoring a baby's growth through head circumference or ensuring a vehicle fits under a bridge, measuring physical quantities correctly is a fundamental skill in both science and daily life.

## Key definitions

- **Physical quantity**: A quantity that can be measured, consisting of a numerical magnitude and a unit.
- **Base quantity**: One of seven fundamental quantities, such as length, mass, and time.
- **Derived quantity**: A quantity obtained by combining base quantities (e.g. speed = length ÷ time).
- **SI units**: The International System of Units used universally by scientists.
- **Precision**: The smallest division on a measuring instrument (e.g. 1 mm on a metre rule).
- **Parallax error**: An error when the eye is not perpendicular to the scale being read.
- **Period ($T$)**: The time for one complete oscillation of a pendulum (SI unit: second, s).
### Common quantities and SI units
| Quantity | SI unit | Symbol |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Length | metre | m |
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Time | second | s |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Electric current | ampere | A |

- Use a metre rule or measuring tape.
- Read the scale with your eye directly above the mark (perpendicular to the ruler) to avoid parallax error.
- Record the value to the precision of the instrument (e.g. nearest mm).

**Measuring mass**

- Use a top-pan balance or electronic balance.
- Mass is measured in kilograms (kg) in SI units; grams (g) must be converted ($1000\ \text{g} = 1\ \text{kg}$).
- Mass is **not** the same as weight — weight is a force (newtons).

**Measuring time**

- Use a stopwatch or timer.
- For regular oscillations, a **simple pendulum** gives more reliable average times — measure the time for 10 or 20 complete swings and divide by the number of oscillations.
- **Period** $T$ = time for one complete swing (there and back).

## Steps / method

**Exam method for a reliable pendulum period measurement:**

Set up a simple pendulum with a fixed length (e.g. 1 m string).

Displace the bob slightly and release — keep the swing angle small.

Time 20 complete oscillations using a stopwatch.

Divide total time by 20 to find the period $T$.

Repeat and average to reduce random error.

## Common mistakes

- **Confusing mass and weight**: Mass (kg) is the amount of matter; weight (N) is the gravitational force ($W = mg$).

- **Parallax error**: Reading a ruler from an angle — always read at eye level, perpendicular to the scale.

- **Single timing**: Using one swing only — always time multiple oscillations and divide.

## Examiner tip

- Always state the **unit** with your numerical answer.

- When converting grams to kilograms, **divide** by 1000 (not multiply).

- For pendulum experiments, explain that averaging over many oscillations reduces reaction-time error.

## Worked example — Converting units

Question: A block has a mass of 250 g. Express this mass in SI units.

1. Recall the conversion: $1000\ \text{g} = 1\ \text{kg}$.

2. Divide by 1000: $\frac{250\ \text{g}}{1000} = 0.25\ \text{kg}$.

**Final answer:** $0.25\ \text{kg}$.

## Worked example — Pendulum period

Question: A student times 20 complete swings of a pendulum and records 40.0 s. Calculate the period.

1. Total time for 20 oscillations $= 40.0\ \text{s}$.

2. Period $T = \frac{40.0}{20} = 2.0\ \text{s}$.

**Final answer:** $T = 2.0\ \text{s}$.
