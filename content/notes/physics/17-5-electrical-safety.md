## Core idea

Electrical safety means understanding mains electricity hazards and the features that protect users and property from fire and electric shock.

## Common electrical hazards

- **Damaged insulation**: Worn PVC or rubber exposes live wires — risk of shock.
- **Overheating cables**: Excessive current from overloading a socket.
- **Damp conditions**: Water conducts — using appliances near water increases shock risk.

## Mains circuit wires

| Wire | Colour | Role |
|------|--------|------|
| Live (L) | Brown | ~240 V to the appliance; fuses and switches fitted here |
| Neutral (N) | Blue | ~0 V return path to supply |
| Earth (E) | Green/yellow | Low-resistance safety wire to metal casing |

## Safety features

**Trip switches (circuit breakers)** — automatically cut supply on a current surge; can be reset after fixing the fault (unlike fuses).

**Fuses** — thin wire melts when current exceeds the rated value, opening the circuit. Fuse rating should be slightly above normal operating current. Use $I = P/V$ — e.g. a 2880 W heater on 240 V draws 12 A, so a 13 A fuse is suitable.

**Switch placement** — switches must be on the **live** wire. On the neutral wire, the appliance can remain at high voltage when switched off.

**Earthing** — if the live wire touches the metal casing, current flows through the low-resistance earth wire (not a person), blowing the fuse and isolating the appliance.

**Three-pin plugs and double insulation** — three-pin plugs house the fuse and connect all three wires. Double-insulated appliances have plastic casings, need no earth wire, and carry the double-square symbol.
