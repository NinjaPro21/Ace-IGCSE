## Core idea

Have you ever tried to balance a ruler on your finger? You will find that there is only one specific spot — the very middle — where it does not topple over. Every object behaves as if all its weight is concentrated at a single point called the **centre of gravity**. Understanding this concept is essential for designing stable vehicles, furniture, and structures.

## Key definitions

- **Centre of Gravity (CG)**: The point through which the weight of an object acts.
- **Weight**: The gravitational force acting on an object that has mass (SI unit: newton, N).
- **Stability**: A measure of an object's ability to return to its original position after being slightly displaced.
- **Equilibrium**: A state where there is no resultant force and no resultant moment acting on an object.
- **Plane Lamina**: A flat, thin sheet of material.

## Key formulas

**Moment of weight**

$\text{Moment} = W \times d$

Where $W$ and $d$ is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the weight.

**Condition for equilibrium**

$\text{Resultant moment} = 0$

This occurs when an object is supported directly at its centre of gravity.

## Graphs & diagrams

**Locating the centre of gravity**

- **Regular shapes**: For uniform objects like circles, rectangles, or spheres, the CG is at the geometrical centre.
- **Outside the object**: The CG does not have to be inside the material. For a ring, the CG is at the centre of the hole.

**Types of equilibrium**

- **Stable**: The CG rises when tilted; the line of action of weight stays within the base.
- **Unstable**: The CG drops when tilted; the line of action of weight falls outside the base, causing it to topple.
- **Neutral**: The CG remains at the same height when the object is moved (e.g., a ball rolling).

## Steps / method

Method for finding the CG of an irregular plane lamina:

Preparation: Make three small holes near the edges of the lamina, spaced far apart.

Suspension: Hang the lamina from a pin through one hole so it can swing freely.

Plumb Line: Hang a plumb line (a weight on a string) from the same pin in front of the lamina.

Marking: Once steady, draw a line on the lamina following the path of the plumb line.

Repetition: Repeat the process for the other two holes.

Intersection: The point where all three lines cross is the centre of gravity.

## Common mistakes

Internal Only: Believing the CG must be "inside" the physical material of an object (remember the ring!).

Ignoring the Pivot: Measuring distances from the "0" mark of a ruler instead of the pivot point during moment calculations.

Mass vs. Weight: Using mass in kg for moment calculations instead of weight in N ($W = mg$).

## Examiner tip

Stability Rules: To increase stability, always suggest keeping the CG as low as possible (mass at the bottom) and the base area as wide as possible.

Resultant Moment: In equilibrium questions, explicitly state that both the resultant force and the resultant moment are zero.

Precision: When drawing plumb lines, ensure the holes in the lamina are small to avoid removing too much mass, which would change the CG.

## Worked example — Finding the Weight of a Ruler

Question: A uniform 100 cm ruler is balanced at the 70 cm mark when a 2.5 N weight is hung at the 80 cm mark. Calculate the weight ($W$) of the ruler.

1. Identify the CG. For a uniform ruler, CG is at the 50 cm mark.

2. Distance from pivot (70 cm) to the weight: $80 - 70 = 10\ \text{cm}$.

3. Clockwise moment: $2.5\ \text{N} \times 10\ \text{cm} = 25\ \text{N cm}$.

4. Distance from pivot to the ruler's CG: $70 - 50 = 20\ \text{cm}$.

5. Apply Principle of Moments: $W \times 20\ \text{cm} = 25\ \text{N cm}$, so $W = 25 / 20 = 1.25\ \text{N}$.

**Final answer:** The weight of the ruler is $1.25\ \text{N}$.

## Worked example — Minibus Stability

Question: A minibus is empty but has heavy luggage on its roof rack. Explain why it must turn corners slowly.

1. Luggage on the roof rack raises the centre of gravity of the vehicle.

2. When turning, the bus tilts. Because the CG is high, a small tilt can move the line of action of the weight outside the base area.

3. The high CG makes the bus unstable; turning fast could cause it to topple as the weight creates a turning moment away from the base.

### Common Trap

Students often focus only on the weight. However, it is the **position** of the CG that determines whether the resulting moment will cause the vehicle to return or topple.
