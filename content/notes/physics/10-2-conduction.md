## Core idea

Conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a solid from a hotter region to a cooler region, without any bulk movement of the material itself. Metals are excellent conductors; non-metals and gases are generally poor conductors (insulators).

## Key definitions

- **Conduction**: Transfer of thermal energy through a medium without bulk movement of the material.
- **Thermal conductor**: Material that transfers thermal energy quickly (e.g. metals).
- **Thermal insulator**: Material that transfers thermal energy slowly (e.g. wood, plastic, glass, air).
- **Lattice vibration**: Vibration of particles about fixed positions in a solid.
- **Free electrons**: Electrons in metals that move freely and carry kinetic energy rapidly through the material.

## Mechanism of conduction

**In non-metals (insulators)**

- Particles at the hot end vibrate more vigorously.
- They collide with neighbours, passing on kinetic energy.
- Energy is transferred slowly from particle to particle along the material.

**In metals (conductors)**

- Both lattice vibrations **and** free electron diffusion transfer energy.
- Free electrons at the hot end gain kinetic energy and move quickly to cooler regions.
- They collide with atoms in cooler parts, transferring energy much faster than vibration alone.

**In liquids and gases**

- Generally poor conductors — particles are far apart, so collisions are infrequent.
- Convection (not conduction) is the main transfer method in fluids.

## Steps / method

**Explaining conduction in metals:**

1. State that the hot end has particles (and electrons) with more kinetic energy.
2. Explain that free electrons move to cooler regions and collide with atoms there.
3. Conclude that kinetic energy is transferred along the metal without the metal moving as a whole.

**Proving water is a poor conductor (experiment):**

1. Heat the **top** of a test tube of water with an ice cube at the bottom (held by a wire gauze).
2. Observe: water at the top boils while ice at the bottom stays frozen.
3. Conclude: conduction through water is very slow.

## Common mistakes

**Metals feel colder**: Metal is not actually colder than wood at room temperature — it conducts energy away from your hand **faster**, so it *feels* colder.

**Conduction in fluids**: Water and air are poor conductors; convection dominates in fluids.

**Particles moving along**: In conduction, energy is passed between particles — the particles themselves do not travel along the rod (except free electrons in metals).

## Examiner tip

For "why does metal feel colder?" questions, compare **rates** of conduction at the **same temperature**. Mention free electrons in metals for full marks.

## Worked example — Metal vs Plastic at Same Temperature

Question: A metal door handle and a plastic handle are both at $20^\circ\text{C}$. Explain why the metal handle feels colder to the touch.

1. **Same temperature**: Both are at $20^\circ\text{C}$, so there is the same temperature difference with your warm hand ($\approx 37^\circ\text{C}$).

2. **Different conductivity**: Metal is a good conductor; plastic is an insulator.

3. **Energy transfer rate**: Metal conducts thermal energy away from your hand much faster than plastic.

4. **Conclusion**: Your hand loses energy quickly to the metal, so it feels colder.

**Final answer:** The metal feels colder because it conducts thermal energy away from your hand faster, even though both handles are at the same temperature.
