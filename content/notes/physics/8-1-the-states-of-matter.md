## Core idea

From the air you breathe to the ice in your glass, matter is all around you in different forms. Understanding how substances switch between solid, liquid, and gas is essential for everything from engineering stable railway tracks to understanding the natural water cycle. By mastering the properties of these states, you can predict how materials will behave when they are heated, cooled, or squeezed under pressure.

## Key definitions

- **Solid**: A state of matter with a fixed shape and a fixed volume.
- **Liquid**: A state of matter with a fixed volume but no fixed shape; it takes the shape of its container.
- **Gas**: A state of matter with no fixed shape or volume; it is easily compressed.
- **Melting point**: The specific temperature at which a solid turns into a liquid.
- **Boiling point**: The specific temperature at which a liquid turns into a gas.
- **Density ($\rho$)**: The mass per unit volume of a substance (SI unit: kilogram per cubic metre, $\text{kg/m}^3$).
### Particle model comparison
| State | Arrangement | Movement | Forces |
|-------|-------------|----------|--------|
| **Solid** | Closely packed, regular pattern | Vibrate about fixed positions | Strong — particles very close |
| **Liquid** | Random, slightly further apart | Slide past each other | Moderate — still close |
| **Gas** | Far apart, random | Move quickly in all directions | Weak — particles far apart |

## Syllabus wording

- Know the properties of solids, liquids, and gases.
- Know the terms for changes of state between solids, liquids, and gases (melting, boiling, condensation, and freezing/solidification).
- Understand that changing state is a physical change because no new substances are formed.

## Key formulas

**Density:**

$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$

Applies to all states of matter to determine how much mass is packed into a given space 2

**Temperature conversion:**

$T\ (\text{in K}) = \theta\ (\text{in }^\circ\text{C}) + 273$

Used to convert between the Celsius scale and the absolute Kelvin scale.

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Heating curves**: A graph of temperature against time features horizontal plateaus where temperature stays constant during melting and boiling, even though thermal energy is still being added.
- **Cooling curves**: The reverse of a heating curve — horizontal sections during condensation and solidification as the substance releases energy.
- **State transitions**: Red arrows usually indicate energy input (melting, boiling); blue arrows indicate energy loss (condensation, freezing).

## Steps / method

**Exam method for identifying changes of state:** 1. **Identify the initial and final states:** Is the substance moving from a more ordered state (solid) to a less ordered one (gas), or vice versa? 2. **Determine energy flow:** Heating means absorbing energy (melting or boiling). Cooling means releasing energy (condensing or freezing). 3. **Locate the transition point:** On a temperature–time graph, look for flat horizontal lines to find melting point or boiling point. 4. **Check for exceptions:** Ice is less dense than water — most other solids are denser than their liquid forms.

## Common mistakes

- **Expanding particles:** Particles stay the same size; only the space between them increases.

- **Temperature during change:** Temperature stays constant during the actual change of state.

- **Gas vs vapour:** Steam is an invisible gas; visible mist is condensed liquid droplets.

## Examiner tip

- **Unit conversion:** To convert density from $\text{g/cm}^3$ to $\text{kg/m}^3$, multiply by 1000.

- **Precise language:** Use "compressible" when describing gases to earn full marks.

## Worked example 1 — Calculating Density

Question: A metal block has a mass of 15.75 kg and a volume of $0.002\ \text{m}^3$. Calculate its density in SI units 2

Identify variables: $m = 15.75\ \text{kg}$, $V = 0.002\ \text{m}^3$.

Apply formula: $\rho = m/V$. So $\rho = 15.75 / 0.002 = 7875\ \text{kg/m}^3$.

**Final answer:** $7875\ \text{kg/m}^3$ (close to the density of iron).

## Worked example 2 — Volume and State

Question: A sealed container holds $100\ \text{cm}^3$ of a substance. When heated, it expands to fill a $500\ \text{cm}^3$ container completely. Identify the original state and the final state. 1. **Analyse volume:** The substance changed volume to fill a larger container.

 2. **Identify state:** Only a gas has no fixed volume and can expand to fill any space.

 3. **Conclusion:** The substance was likely a liquid (fixed volume) that turned into a gas (no fixed volume) through boiling or evaporation. ### Trap

** Solids and liquids expand slightly when heated, but only gases change volume significantly to match the container.
