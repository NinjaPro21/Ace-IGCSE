## Core idea

Whether it is a powerful rocket engine lifting a satellite or a light bulb illuminating a room, the concept of power allows us to measure how quickly work is being done or how fast energy is being transferred. In Physics, "powerful" doesn't just mean "strong"—it refers to the rate of energy conversion. Understanding power is essential for engineers to design efficient machinery and for consumers to understand the energy demands of their household appliances.

## Key definitions

- **Power ($P$)**: The work done or energy transferred per unit time.
- **Watt ($W$)**: The SI unit of power. One watt is defined as the work done or energy transferred at a rate of one joule per second ($1\ \text{W} = 1\ \text{J/s}$).
- **Work Done ($W$)**: The product of a constant force and the distance moved in the direction of that force. (SI unit: joule, J).
- **Energy Transferred ($\Delta E$)**: The total amount of energy converted from one form to another during a process. (SI unit: joule, J).

## Key formulas

**Power from Work Done**

$P = \frac{W}{t}$

Applies when a constant force is used to move an object over a period of time.

**Power from Energy Change**

$P = \frac{\Delta E}{t}$

Applies to any system where energy is being converted, such as an electrical heater or a falling object.

**Electrical Power**

$P = IV$

Applies specifically to electrical circuits where $I$ is current in A and $V$ is potential difference in V.

## Graphs & diagrams

- Two people of equal weight climb the same flight of stairs.
- The person who reaches the top in less time transfers the same energy but at a greater rate — they are more powerful.
- Power = energy transferred ÷ time; less time means greater power for the same work.

**Power and efficiency**

- A 40 W lamp uses 40 J of electrical energy every second.
- If only 10% is converted to light, useful power = 4 W and wasted power = 36 W.
- Efficiency can also be expressed using power: $\text{efficiency} = \dfrac{\text{useful power}}{\text{input power}} \times 100\%$.

## Steps / method

Exam method for calculating power in mechanical systems:

1. **Identify the force**: Determine the constant force ($F$) being applied (often the weight of an object, $W = mg$).
2. **Determine the distance**: Find the distance ($s$) moved in the direction of the force.
3. **Calculate work done**: Use $W = Fs$ to find the total energy required.
4. **Identify the time**: Find the time ($t$) taken for the action, ensuring it is in seconds.
5. **Calculate power**: Divide the work done by the time taken ($P = W/t$) and state the answer in Watts (W).

## Common mistakes

**Unit confusion**: Using kilowatts (kW) in the formula $P = W/t$ instead of converting them to Watts (W) first.

**Time units**: Failing to convert minutes or hours into seconds, which is required for the Watt definition ($1\ \text{J/s}$).

**Work vs. power**: Confusing the total energy spent (Joules) with the rate of spending (Watts).

## Examiner tip

**Power–time product**: Remember that $P \times t$ always gives you the energy converted or work done.

**Efficiency units**: When calculating efficiency using power, ensure both input and output values are in the same unit (e.g., both in Watts).

**Standard $g$**: Use $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$ (or $10\ \text{m/s}^2$ if specified) to find the weight force before calculating mechanical power.

## Worked example — Climbing Stairs

Question: Eugene weighs $450\ \text{N}$ and runs up ten steps. Each step is $0.20\ \text{m}$ high. Calculate Eugene's power if he takes five seconds to run up the steps at a constant speed.

1. Find total vertical distance: $s = 10 \times 0.20 = 2.0\ \text{m}$.

2. Calculate work done against gravity: $W = Fs = 450 \times 2.0 = 900\ \text{J}$.

3. Apply power formula: $P = \frac{900}{5} = 180\ \text{W}$.

**Final answer:** $180\ \text{W}$.

## Worked example — Efficiency and Power

Question: A filament lamp rated at $40\ \text{W}$ converts $10\%$ of its energy into light. Calculate the useful light energy produced in 5 minutes.

1. Convert time to SI units: $t = 5 \times 60 = 300\ \text{s}$.

2. Find total energy used: $E = P \times t = 40 \times 300 = 12\,000\ \text{J}$.

3. Apply efficiency: useful energy = $10\% \times 12\,000 = 1200\ \text{J}$.

**Final answer:** $1200\ \text{J}$ of light energy.

### Common Trap

Forgetting to convert minutes to seconds.

If you use "5" instead of "300," your energy value will be 60 times too small.
