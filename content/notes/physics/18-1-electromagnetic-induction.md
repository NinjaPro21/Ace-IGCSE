## Core idea

Electromagnetic induction is the process where an induced e.m.f. (electromotive force) is produced in a conductor due to a changing magnetic field. This discovery, made by Michael Faraday in 1831, demonstrated that while a current produces a magnetic field, the converse is also true: a changing magnetic field can produce an electric current.

Faraday’s Experiment and Observations

Faraday used a solenoid (a cylindrical coil of wire) connected to a galvanometer (a sensitive instrument for detecting current) and a permanent bar magnet to test this effect. His key observations were:

Moving the magnet into the solenoid: The galvanometer needle deflected in one direction, showing an induced current was flowing.

Withdrawing the magnet: The needle deflected in the opposite direction.

Stationary magnet: If the magnet was held still inside the solenoid, there was no deflection, meaning no current was induced.

From this, he concluded that relative movement between the conductor and the magnetic field is required to induce an e.m.f..

The Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

Two primary laws govern this phenomenon: Faraday’s Law

This law relates to the magnitude of the induction. It states that the magnitude of the induced e.m.f. is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux (the measure of the magnetic field quantity) in the circuit.

Lenz’s Law

This law relates to the direction of the induction. It states that the direction of the induced e.m.f. and current is always such that its magnetic effect opposes the motion or change that produced it.

Example: If you push the North (N) pole of a magnet into a coil, the induced current will create an N pole at that end of the coil to repel the incoming magnet.

Example: If you pull the North (N) pole out, the induced current creates a South (S) pole at that end to attract the departing magnet.

Factors Affecting the Magnitude of Induced e.m.f.

The strength of the induced e.m.f. can be increased by: Increasing the number of turns in the solenoid (coil).

Increasing the strength of the permanent magnet.

Increasing the speed of the relative motion between the magnet and the coil.

Conservation of Energy

Lenz’s Law is a specific example of the principle of conservation of energy. If the induced current did not oppose the motion, it would create an attractive force that would pull the magnet in faster and faster without any external work, which would violate the law that energy cannot be created or destroyed.
