## Core idea

In 1820, Hans Christian Oersted discovered that an electric current flowing through a wire produces a magnetic field around it. This discovery established the relationship between electricity and magnetism, known as electromagnetism.

Magnetic Field Around a Straight Wire

When current flows through a straight wire, it creates a magnetic field with specific characteristics:

Pattern: The magnetic field consists of concentric circles centred on the wire.

Strength: The field is strongest near the wire, where the field lines are most closely packed. The strength increases if the magnitude of the current is increased.

Direction: The direction of the field lines reverses if the direction of the current is reversed.

The Right-Hand Grip Rule (Straight Wire)

This rule is used to determine the direction of the magnetic field around a wire: Imagine gripping the wire with your right hand.

Point your thumb in the direction of the conventional current.

The direction in which your fingers curl indicates the direction of the magnetic field (clockwise or anticlockwise).

Magnetic Field Around a Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire. When current passes through it, the combined magnetic effect of all the loops creates a distinct pattern:

Pattern: The field resembles that of a bar magnet, with a North pole at one end and a South pole at the other.

Internal Field: The field lines inside the solenoid are nearly parallel and uniform, indicating a strong, steady field.

Increasing Strength: The magnetic field of a solenoid can be strengthened by: Increasing the current.

Increasing the number of turns per unit length.

Inserting a soft iron core into the centre to concentrate the field lines.

The Right-Hand Grip Rule (Solenoid)

For a solenoid, the grip rule is applied differently: Curl your fingers in the direction of the conventional current flowing through the loops.

Your thumb then points toward the North pole of the solenoid.

Applications of Electromagnetism

The Electrical Relay

A relay uses a low-current primary circuit to switch a high-current secondary circuit on or off.

Mechanism: When the primary circuit is closed, an electromagnet attracts an iron lever. This movement pushes contacts together to complete the secondary circuit, which may power a heavy-duty device like a motor.

Advantage: It allows a user to control high-voltage or dangerous equipment remotely and safely.

Moving-Coil Loudspeaker

A loudspeaker converts electrical signals into sound using an electromagnet.

Mechanism: A coil of wire (the moving coil) is placed within the field of a strong permanent magnet.

Vibration: When an alternating current (a.c.) flows through the coil, it becomes a temporary magnet that is rapidly attracted to and repelled by the permanent magnet. This causes the attached diaphragm to vibrate, producing sound waves at the same frequency as the a.c. supply.
