## Core idea

In a series circuit, components are connected one after another in a single loop. This arrangement means there is only one path through which an electric charge can flow.

Combined Electromotive Force (e.m.f.)

When energy sources, such as dry cells, are arranged in series, the resultant e.m.f. is the sum of the individual e.m.f.s of the cells. For example, two 1.5 V cells connected in series provide a combined e.m.f. of 3.0 V.

Current in a Series Circuit

The current at every point in a given series circuit is the same. Because there is only one path, no charge can escape or be introduced at any point; this is known as the conservation of charge. Whether you measure the current before or after a resistor, the reading remains identical.

**Potential Difference (p.d.)**

The total potential difference ($V_E$) across all components in a series circuit is equal to the sum of the individual p.d.s across each component.

Formula: $V_E = V_1 + V_2 + \dots + V_n$

Energy Balance: The e.m.f. ($E$) provided by the electrical source is equal to the total p.d. ($V_E$) converted by the components.

Combined Resistance

For resistors connected in series, the combined (or effective) resistance ($R$) is the sum of all individual resistances.

Formula: $R = R_1 + R_2 + \dots + R_n$

Logic: Because the same current ($I$) must pass through every resistor, the total work done per unit charge ($V = IR$) is the sum of the work done for each part ($IR_1 + IR_2 \dots$).

Key Characteristics

Resistance and p.d.: In a series circuit, the component with the larger resistance will have a greater potential difference across it.

Circuit Failure: If one component in a series circuit fails (breaks the loop), the entire circuit becomes an open circuit and current stops flowing to all other components.
