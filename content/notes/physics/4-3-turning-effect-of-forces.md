## Core idea

Whether you are swinging open a heavy door, tightening a bolt with a wrench, or balancing on a see-saw, you are using the turning effect of forces. In physics, this is measured as a **moment**. Understanding moments is essential for architects designing stable buildings and engineers creating everything from simple cranes to complex engines.

## Key definitions

- **Moment of a force**: The product of the force and the perpendicular distance from the pivot. (SI unit: newton metre, $N\ m$).
- **Pivot**: The fixed location about which an object turns.
- **Principle of Moments**: When an object is in equilibrium, the sum of the clockwise moments about a pivot equals the sum of the anticlockwise moments.
- **Equilibrium**: A state where there is no resultant force and no resultant moment acting on an object.

## Key formulas

**Moment of a force**

$\text{Moment} = F \times d$

Where $F$ is the force in newtons (N) and $d$ is the perpendicular distance from the pivot in metres (m).

**Principle of moments**

$\text{Total clockwise moments} = \text{Total anticlockwise moments}$

Applies only when an object is in equilibrium (stationary or rotating at a constant rate).

## Steps / method

Method for solving moments and equilibrium problems:

Identify the pivot: Determine the point about which the object turns.

Identify all forces: Note every force acting on the object, including its own weight acting at the centre of gravity.

Measure perpendicular distances: Find the distance from the pivot to the line of action for each force.

Classify moments: Determine which forces create clockwise turns and which create anticlockwise turns.

Apply the Principle of Moments: Set the sum of clockwise moments equal to the sum of anticlockwise moments and solve for the unknown.

## Common mistakes

Wrong Distance: Using the distance along a tilted beam instead of the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the force.

Ignoring Weight: Forgetting to include the weight of the beam itself if the question does not state it is "light" or "massless".

Unit Errors: Using distances in cm and forces in N to get "N m" without converting cm to m.

Force vs. Moment: Confusing the force applied (N) with the turning effect ($N\ m$).

## Examiner tip

Significant Figures: Always provide final answers to 2 or 3 significant figures.

Show Formulae: Write the $\text{Moment} = F \times d$ formula before substituting values to secure method marks even if your calculation is wrong.

Check Equilibrium: In questions asking for conditions of equilibrium, you must state that both the resultant force AND the resultant moment are zero.

## Worked example — Opening a Door

Question: The minimum moment required to open a door is $20.5\ \text{N m}$. If a person applies a force of $50\ \text{N}$ at the handle, calculate the minimum distance the handle must be from the hinge (pivot).

1. Use the formula: $\text{Moment} = F \times d$ when $2$.

2. Substitute values: $d = 20.5\ \text{N m} / 50\ \text{N} = 0.41\ \text{m}$.

**Final answer:** The handle must be at least $0.41\ \text{m}$ from the hinge.

## Worked example — The Fishing Rod

Question: A man holds a light fishing rod horizontally. A $30\ \text{N}$ fish hangs from the end $1.2\ \text{m}$ from his right hand. His right hand acts as the pivot and his left hand applies an upward force $F$ at $0.3\ \text{m}$ from the pivot. Calculate force $F$.

1. **Clockwise moment (fish):** $30\ \text{N} \times 1.2\ \text{m} = 36\ \text{N m}$.

2. **Anticlockwise moment:** $F \times 0.3\ \text{m}$.

3. **Apply the principle of moments:** $0.3F = 36$.

4. **Solve:** $F = 36 / 0.3 = 120\ \text{N}$.

**Final answer:** $F = 120\ \text{N}$.

### Common Trap

Students often use the distance between the two hands rather than the distance from the pivot. Always measure from the pivot point.
