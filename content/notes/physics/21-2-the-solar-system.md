## Core idea

Around 4.6 billion years ago, our Solar System was nothing more than a swirling cloud of dust and gas. Today, it is a complex neighborhood dominated by the Sun's massive gravity, which keeps eight diverse planets, dozens of moons, and countless asteroids and comets in perfectly balanced elliptical orbits. Understanding the mechanics of the Solar System is not just about identifying planets; it is about mastering the laws of gravity and energy conservation that govern the motion of every object in the vacuum of space.

## Key definitions

- **Sun**: The medium-sized star at the center of our Solar System, containing 99% of its total matter.
- **Nebula**: A large cloud of gas (mainly hydrogen) and dust from which stars and solar systems are formed.
- **Protostar**: A hot, spinning mass of gas formed at the center of a nebula before nuclear fusion begins.
- **Accretion Disc**: A swirling disc of gas and dust around a protostar where matter clumps together to form planets.
- **Planet**: A significant spherical body that orbits a star; our system has four inner rocky planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) and four outer gas and ice giants (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune).
- **Moon**: A natural satellite that orbits a planet.
- **Asteroid**: An irregularly shaped lump of rock left over from the formation of the Solar System, typically found in the belt between Mars and Jupiter.
- **Comet**: A lump of frozen gas and dust that orbits the Sun in a highly elliptical path.
- **Dwarf Planet**: A spherical body less massive than the eight main planets, such as Pluto or Ceres.

## Key formulas

**Average Orbital Speed**

$v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$

Applies to objects in circular or nearly circular orbits, where $r$ is the orbital radius and $T$ is the orbital period.

**Travel Time (for light or signals)**

$t = \frac{s}{v}$

Used to calculate how long light ($v = 3 \times 10^{8}\ m/s$) takes to travel between objects in space.

**Hubble’s Law (Cosmological Context)**

$v = H_0 d$

Applies to the recessional velocity of galaxies moving away from Earth, where $H_0$ is the Hubble constant.

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Elliptical Orbits**: Most orbits in the Solar System are ellipses rather than perfect circles. For comets, the Sun is located at one "focus" of the ellipse rather than the exact center.
- **Comet Velocity and Energy**: A diagram of a comet's orbit shows it moving fastest when closest to the Sun (maximum kinetic energy) and slowest when furthest away (maximum gravitational potential energy).
- **Distance vs. Orbital Time**: A graph of planetary data shows that as the distance from the Sun increases, the time to complete an orbit increases significantly because the Sun's gravitational field strength decreases.

## Steps / method

Exam Method for Analyzing Planetary Data:

Identify the Planet Type: Determine if the planet is rocky (inner) or gaseous (outer) based on its density and distance.

Evaluate Gravitational Field: Use the planet's mass to determine its gravitational field strength ($g$); higher mass generally means higher $g$.

Calculate Orbital Speed: Use $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, ensuring units for $r$ (metres) and $T$ (seconds) are converted correctly.

Compare Light Travel Time: Use $t = \frac{s}{c}$ to find the time delay for signals or light to reach Earth from specific planets.

## Common mistakes

Circular Orbits: Assuming all orbits are perfect circles; they are actually ellipses.

Formation Speed: Believing the Solar System formed quickly; it actually took millions of years for the nebula to collapse and clump.

GPE Confusion: Thinking GPE is highest when the comet is near the Sun. GPE is actually at its maximum when the object is at its furthest distance from the Sun.

## Examiner tip

Sun's Composition: If asked what the Sun is made of, specify hydrogen and helium.

Gravitational Strength: Always link the strength of a planet's gravitational field to its mass and the distance from its center.

Energy in Orbits: Use the phrase "conservation of energy" when explaining why objects speed up as they approach the Sun in an elliptical orbit.

## Worked example — Sunlight Travel Time

Question: The average distance from the Sun to the Earth is $150$ million kilometers. Calculate how long it takes for sunlight to reach Earth in minutes and seconds. (Speed of light $c = 3 \times 10^{8}\ m/s$).

Convert distance to SI units:

$$
150\,000\,000\ \text{km} = 1.5 \times 10^{11}\ \text{m}
$$

Apply the formula:

$$
t = \frac{1.5 \times 10^{11}}{3 \times 10^{8}} = 500\ \text{s}
$$

Convert to minutes: 500 s = 8 minutes and 20 seconds.

**Final answer:** 8 minutes 20 seconds.

## Worked example — Comet Energy Changes

Question: A comet is moving in a highly elliptical orbit. Describe the energy changes that occur as it moves from its furthest point (aphelion) toward the Sun.

Identify the force: The Sun’s gravitational field pulls the comet inward.

Describe the energy transfer: Gravitational potential energy (GPE) is converted into kinetic energy (KE) as the comet accelerates.

State the result: The comet travels at its maximum speed when it is closest to the Sun.

### Common Trap

Students often think the comet has more total energy when it's faster. The truth is the total energy remains constant (ignoring gas evaporation), but the ratio of KE to GPE shifts.
