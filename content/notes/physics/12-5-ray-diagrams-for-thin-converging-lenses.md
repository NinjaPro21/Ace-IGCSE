## Core idea

The position and nature of an image formed by a thin converging lens depend on the distance of the object from the lens relative to its focal length. By using the predictable behaviour of specific light rays, we can construct ray diagrams to precisely locate these images.

Predictable Light Rays

Three particular rays are used to construct ray diagrams because their paths through a thin converging lens are known: Ray 1: A ray passing through the optical centre ($C$) of the lens travels in a straight line without bending.

Ray 2: A ray travelling parallel to the principal axis is refracted so that it passes through the principal focus ($F$) on the opposite side.

Ray 3: A ray passing through the principal focus ($F$) before hitting the lens is refracted so that it travels parallel to the principal axis.

Drawing Ray Diagrams

To find the image position, follow these general steps:

Set up the diagram: Draw the principal axis, the lens (represented by a double-headed arrow), the optical centre ($C$), and the focal points ($F$) on both sides.

Place the object: Draw the object ($O$) as a vertical arrow at the specified distance ($u$) from the lens.

Trace the rays: Draw any two of the three predictable rays from the tip of the object.

Locate the image: The point where these rays (or their virtual extensions) intersect is the tip of the image ($I$).

Key Rule: When $u > f$, the image is real, inverted, and on the opposite side of the lens. When $u \leq f$, the image is virtual, upright, and on the same side as the object.

Practical Applications

Magnifying Glass: To use a converging lens as a magnifier, the object must be placed at a distance less than the focal length ($u < f$). This produces a magnified, upright, and virtual image.

Visual Correction: Long-sightedness: Occurs when the eye cannot focus on close objects because rays reach the retina before converging. Converging lenses are used to partially converge rays before they enter the eye, allowing them to focus on the retina.

Short-sightedness: Occurs when rays from distant objects focus in front of the retina. Diverging (concave) lenses are used to spread the rays before they reach the eye, moving the focus back onto the retina.
