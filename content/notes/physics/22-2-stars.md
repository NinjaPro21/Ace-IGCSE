## Core idea

Stars are the fundamental building blocks of the Universe and the chemical factories where all elements are forged. From our own medium-sized Sun to the cataclysmic explosions of massive supernovae, the life cycle of a star determines the distribution of matter across space. Understanding these cosmic cycles is not just about looking at distant lights; it is about discovering our own origins, as the very atoms in your body were once created in the heart of a dying star.

## Key definitions

- **Billion**: A numerical value representing one thousand million ($1000000000$).
- **Galaxy**: A group of many billions of stars; our Sun belongs to the Milky Way galaxy.
- **Light-year (ly)**: The distance that light travels in one year in the vacuum of space, which is a scalar quantity (SI unit: metre, m).
- **Nebula**: A large cloud of dust and gas (mainly hydrogen) in space.
- **Protostar**: A hot, spinning mass of gas formed at the centre of a nebula before nuclear fusion begins.
- **Red Giant**: An average-sized star that has used up its hydrogen, causing it to expand and cool.
- **Supernova**: A violent explosion of a massive star that produces elements heavier than iron.
- **Black Hole**: An object so dense that its gravitational field prevents even light from escaping.

## Key formulas

**Distance in a Light-year**

$s = vt$

Used to calculate the distance light travels in one year, where $v = c$ ($3 \times 10^{8}$ m/s) and $t$ is the number of seconds in a year.

**Constant Light-year Value**

$$
1\ \text{ly} = 9.5 \times 10^{15}\ \text{m}
$$

Applies when converting astronomical distances into standard SI metres.

## Steps / method

Exam Method for Describing Star Life Cycles:

Initial Formation: State that stars form from a nebula (gas/dust cloud) that collapses due to gravitational attraction.

Fusion Start: Describe the protostar heating up until nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium begins.

Stability: Explain that the star remains stable while inward gravity is balanced by outward radiation pressure.

Death of Average Stars: Describe the expansion into a red giant once hydrogen runs out, eventually leaving a white dwarf.

Death of Massive Stars: Describe the expansion into a red supergiant, the explosion of a supernova, and the formation of a neutron star or black hole.

## Common mistakes

Mixing up Giants: Confusing "Red Giant" (average star) with "Red Supergiant" (massive star).

Supernova Origins: Believing the Sun will eventually become a supernova; only massive stars explode this way.

Light-year as Time: Thinking a light-year is a unit of time because it contains the word "year"; it is always a unit of distance.

Fusion vs. Fission: Stating that stars are powered by nuclear fission rather than fusion.

## Examiner tip

Specify Fusion Reactants: When describing a stable star, always use the phrase "fusion of hydrogen into helium" to ensure you gain the mark.

Element Creation: Remember that elements heavier than hydrogen but up to iron are made in the cores of stars, but elements more massive than iron are only created in a supernova.

Force Balance: If asked why a star is "stable," mention the balance between inward gravitational pull and outward thermal/radiation pressure.

## Worked example — Calculating Distance in Metres

Question: The star Proxima Centauri is approximately 4.2 light-years away from Earth. Calculate this distance in metres.

Identify the value of 1 light-year: $9.5 \times 10^{15}$ m.

Multiply the distance by the value:

$$
4.2 \times (9.5 \times 10^{15}) = 3.99 \times 10^{16}\ \text{m}
$$

**Final answer:** $3.99 \times 10^{16}$ m.

## Worked example — Comparing Star Lifespans

Question: Explain why a massive blue supergiant has a shorter lifespan than an average star like the Sun.

Mass and Temperature: Massive stars are much hotter and larger than average stars.

Rate of Fusion: Because they are hotter, they consume their hydrogen fuel much faster than average stars.

Lifespan Comparison: While average stars live for billions of years, massive stars live for only millions of years.

### Trap

Students often think more mass means more fuel and thus a longer life. The trap is that the increased pressure and temperature in massive stars force the fuel to burn at a significantly faster rate.
