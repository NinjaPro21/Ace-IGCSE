## Core idea

Electric circuits serve as a means to transfer energy from a power source (such as a battery or the mains supply) to various circuit components. As current flows through these components, electrical energy is dissipated—converted into other forms like heat or light—and then transferred to the surroundings.

Electrical Power ($P$)

Power is defined as the amount of work done or energy transferred per unit time.

SI Unit: The watt (W), where $1 \text{ watt} =$1 \text{ joule per second}$($1 \text{ J/s}$)$.

Formula: $P = IV$ Where $P$ is power in watts (W), $I$ is current in amperes (A), and $V$ is potential difference in volts (V).

Electrical Energy ($E$)

The total amount of electrical energy transferred depends on the power of the appliance and how long it is used.

SI Unit: The joule (J).

Formula: $E = Pt = IVt

$$Where$Eis electrical energy in joules (J) andtis time in seconds (s)$.

Calculating the Cost of Electricity

Because the joule is a very small unit of energy, electricity companies use the kilowatt-hour (kWh) to measure and bill for household consumption.

Definition: A kilowatt-hour is the amount of energy used by a $1 \text{ kW}$ appliance when it is switched on for one hour$.

Cost Calculation: \text{Total Cost} = \text{Total Energy used (in kWh)} \times \text{Tariff (price per kWh)}

## Worked example — — Calculating Energy and Cost

Question: An electric kettle rated at $3 \text{ kW} is used for 20 minutes. A 100 \text{ W} light bulb is used for 5 hours$. If the tariff is 27 cents per kWh, calculate the total cost2

Step 1: Find energy for the kettle$.E_1 =$3 \text{ kW} \times (20/60)\text{ h} = $1 \text{ kWh}$. Step 2: Find energy for the bulb.100 \text{ W} = 0.1 \text{ kW}.E_2 = 0.1 \text{ kW} \times $5 \text{ h} = 0.5 \text{ kWh}$. Step 3: Total energy $= 1 + 0.5 = 1.5 \text{ kWh}$. Step 4: Total cost $= 1.5 \text{ kWh} \times 27 \text{ cents} = 40.5 \text{ cents}. Solution: Approximately$0.41.
