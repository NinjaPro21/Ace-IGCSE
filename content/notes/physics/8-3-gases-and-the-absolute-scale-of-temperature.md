## Core idea

Understanding how gases behave is crucial for everything from inflating a bicycle tyre to predicting weather patterns. By using the kinetic particle model, we can explain how invisible gas particles exert massive forces and how changes in temperature or volume can cause dramatic shifts in pressure. This section also introduces the Kelvin scale, the fundamental temperature scale used by scientists to describe the motion of particles at their most basic level.

## Key definitions

- **Gas Pressure**: The effect of the constant random collisions of many gas particles with the walls of their container, which exerts a large force over the container's surface area.
- **Inverse Proportionality**: A mathematical relationship where one value increases as the other decreases at a constant rate; for example, doubling the volume of a gas halves its pressure (at constant temperature).
- **Absolute Zero**: The lowest possible temperature, occurring at $-273^\circ\text{C}$ or $1 \text{ K}$, where particles have their minimum energy state$.
- **Kelvin scale**: The SI unit scale for temperature ($K$), where a change of one degree Kelvin is identical to a change of one degree Celsius.

## Key formulas

**Celsius to Kelvin Conversion**

$T \text{ (in K)} = \theta \text{ (in } ^\circ\text{C)} + 273$

Used to convert common Celsius readings into the absolute temperature scale required for gas law calculations.

**Pressure-Volume Relationship (Boyle's Law)**

$pV = constant \quad or \quad p_1V_1 = p_2V_2$

Applies to a fixed mass of gas at a constant temperature.

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Pressure–Temperature Graph**: At a constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature in Kelvin, resulting in a straight line passing through the origin.
- **Pressure–Volume Graph**: At a constant temperature, the relationship is an inverse curve; as volume decreases, pressure increases.
Pressure–$1/\text{Volume}$ Graph: Plotting pressure against the reciprocal of volume ($1/V$) results in a straight line, confirming inverse proportionality.
- **Particle Collision Diagram**: Shows gas particles moving randomly and hitting the walls of a container to exert pressure.

## Steps / method

Method for explaining gas pressure changes using the kinetic model:

Link Temperature to Speed: State that a rise in temperature increases the average speed and kinetic energy of the particles.

Frequency and Force: Explain that faster particles collide with the walls more frequently and more vigorously (exerting more force).

Define Pressure: Conclude that this increased total force over a fixed area results in higher pressure.

For Volume Changes: State that a decrease in volume increases the number of particles per unit volume, leading to more frequent collisions with the container walls.

## Common mistakes

Celsius in Proportions: Attempting to use Celsius values directly in proportionality calculations (e.g., assuming doubling $20^\circ\text{C}$ doubles pressure); you must use Kelvin.

Volume-Pressure Confusion: Thinking that an increase in volume will increase pressure; particles have more space, so collisions are less frequent, and pressure decreases.

Force vs. Pressure: Confusing the force of a single collision with total gas pressure. One collision has a tiny force; pressure is the result of millions of frequent collisions.

## Examiner tip

Terminology: When explaining pressure-volume changes, always mention that the temperature remains constant and you are discussing a fixed mass of gas.

Inverse Graphs: If asked to prove inverse proportionality from a graph, note that the graph of $p$ against $V$ is a curve, but $p$ against $1/V$ is a straight line through the origin.

Kelvin Units: Remember that Kelvin is the SI unit for temperature and does not use the "degree" symbol ($^\circ$).

## Worked example — Boyle's Law

Question: A gas cylinder contains $600 \text{ ml}$ of gas at a pressure of $2 \times 10^$7 \text{ Pa}$. Calculate the volume of this gas if the pressure is reduced to atmospheric pressure (1 \times 10^$5 \text{ Pa}$) at a constant temperature$.

Identify the initial ($p_1, V_1$) and final ($p_2$) values: $p_1 = 2 \times 10^$7 \text{ Pa}$,$ V_1 = 600 \text{ ml}$,$ p_2 = 1 \times 10^$5 \text{ Pa}$.

Apply the formula $p_1V_1 = p_2V_2$: $(2 \times 10^{7}) \times 600 = (1 \times 10^{5}) \times V_2$.

## Worked example — Temperature Conversion

Question: The temperature in a lab is $20^\circ\text{C}$. What is this temperature in Kelvin 2.
