## Core idea

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one optical medium to another. This occurs because light travels at different speeds in different transparent materials. For example, light travels at $3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$ in air but slows down to approximately $2.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$ in glass. This change in speed at the boundary of two media causes the light to change direction.

## Key definitions

- **Refraction**: The bending of light as it passes from one optical medium to another.
- **Normal**: The imaginary line perpendicular to the refracting surface at the point of incidence.
- **Angle of incidence ($i$)**: The angle between the incident ray and the normal.
- **Angle of refraction ($r$)**: The angle between the refracted ray and the normal.
- **Refractive Index ($n$)**: A value representing the ratio of the speeds of light in two different regions, specifically the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to the speed of light in the medium.

## Key formulas

**Snell’s Law**

$n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r}$

States that for two given media, the ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is a constant ($n$2.

**Speed and Refractive Index**

$n = \frac{c}{v}$

Where $c$ and $v$ is the speed of light in the medium.

**Real and Apparent Depth**

$n = \frac{real depth}{apparent depth}$

Used to calculate how much shallower an object appears when viewed through a denser medium like water.

Refraction Rules & Behaviours

Toward the Normal: Light bends towards the normal when it enters an optically denser medium at an angle (e.g., air to glass). In this case, $r < i$.

Away from the Normal: Light bends away from the normal when it enters an optically less dense medium at an angle (e.g., glass to air).

Along the Normal: A light ray travelling from one medium to another along the normal (at $0^\circ$ to the normal) is not refracted and passes straight through, though it still undergoes a change in speed.

Principle of Reversibility: A light ray will travel along the same path even if its direction of travel is reversed.

Daily Phenomena "Bent" Objects: A pencil or rod partially immersed in water appears bent at the surface because light reflected from the underwater portion bends away from the normal as it enters the air, reaching our eyes from a different apparent position.

Depth Perception: Swimming pools always appear shallower than they actually are because refraction causes the light rays from the bottom to bend at the surface, making the floor appear closer to the viewer.

## Common mistakes

Density Confusion: Confusing optical density with mass density. A material can be optically denser (higher $n$) even if its mass density is lower (e.g., liquid paraffin is optically denser than water but has a lower mass density).

Calculation Error: When using Snell's Law, ensure you use the sine of the angles ($\sin i$ and $\sin r$), not the angles themselves.
