## Core idea

Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy in the form of invisible waves called infrared radiation. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does not require a medium to travel through and is the only method by which thermal energy can travel through a vacuum, such as the transfer of energy from the Sun to the Earth.

## Key definitions

- **Thermal Radiation**: Also known as infrared radiation; an invisible radiation that carries thermal energy.
- **Absorption**: The process of taking in infrared radiation, which increases an object's temperature.
- **Emission**: The process of giving out infrared radiation, which decreases an object's temperature.
- **Thermal Equilibrium (Radiation)**: Occurs when the rates of emission and absorption are the same, resulting in no temperature change.
Investigating Emission and Absorption
Experiments using temperature sensors and data loggers demonstrate how different surfaces behave: - **Emission Experiment**: Two tins (one dull black, one shiny silver) filled with boiling water show that the dull black tin cools faster, proving it is a better emitter.
- **Absorption Experiment**: Two sensors (one wrapped in black foil, one in silver foil) placed near a light bulb show that the dull black foil heats up faster, proving it is a better absorber.
- **Conclusion**: Dull black surfaces are the best emitters and absorbers of infrared radiation, while shiny silver surfaces are the worst absorbers (and best reflectors) and worst emitters.
Factors Affecting Radiation
- **The rate of thermal energy transfer by radiation depends on three main factors**: - **Surface Colour and Texture**: Dull, black surfaces emit and absorb radiation at a higher rate than shiny, silver surfaces.
- **Surface Temperature**: The higher an object's temperature relative to its surroundings, the higher its rate of infrared emission.
- **Surface Area**: For objects of the same mass and material, the one with a larger surface area will emit or absorb radiation at a higher rate.
Applications and Consequences
- **Greenhouse Effect**: A natural process where the Earth's surface warms through a balance of absorbing solar radiation and emitting its own infrared radiation, some of which is trapped by greenhouse gases.
- **Space Blankets**: Used by marathon runners, these shiny silver blankets reduce thermal energy loss by reflecting heat back to the body and minimizing emission via radiation.
- **Solar Water Heaters**: These are painted black to maximize the absorption of infrared radiation from the Sun.
- **Global Warming**: Increasing greenhouse gases (like CO2 and methane) trap more radiated thermal energy, raising Earth's temperature. Melting snow also worsens this because snow is a good reflector; as it melts, darker land absorbs more radiation.

Applications and Consequences of Thermal Energy Transfer

Thermal energy transfer through conduction, convection, and radiation is not just a theoretical concept; it governs many everyday phenomena and engineering designs. By understanding how heat moves, we can create more efficient homes, safer tools, and better understand our environment.

Conduction: Applications and Consequences

Sensation of Temperature: Metal objects feel colder to the touch than plastic objects even when they are at the same temperature. This is because metal is a good thermal conductor and transfers thermal energy away from your warm hand much faster than plastic, which is an insulator.

Cooking Utensils: Saucepans and frying pans are made of metals like copper, aluminium, or steel to conduct heat quickly from the stove to the food.

Insulated Handles: The handles of these cooking pots are made of wood or plastic to protect your hands from being burnt, as these materials are poor conductors (insulators).

Double-glazing: Windows use a layer of trapped still air between two glass panes because still air is an excellent thermal insulator, reducing heat loss from a building.

Convection: Applications and Consequences

Sea Breezes: Near the coast, a breeze often blows because land heats up and cools down faster than the sea. During the day, warm air over the land rises, and cooler air from the sea moves in to take its place. At night, the process reverses as the land cools more quickly than the water.

Household Appliances:

Air Conditioners: These are installed near the ceiling because cool air is denser and sinks, while the warmer air below rises to be cooled, creating a circulating current that cools the entire room.

Hot Water Radiators: Despite their name, they heat a room mainly by convection. Hot air above the radiator rises, and cooler air is drawn in from the bottom to be heated.

Electric Kettles: The heating element is at the bottom to ensure convection currents heat the water quickly and evenly throughout the container.

Radiation: Applications and Consequences

Global Warming: The Earth is warmed by infrared radiation from the Sun. Greenhouse gases like $CO_2$ and methane trap this heat, and as snow (a good reflector) melts, the darker land and sea absorb even more radiation, accelerating the warming process.

Greenhouses: Glass roofs allow solar radiation to enter, warming the soil and plants. These warmed objects emit longer-wavelength infrared radiation, which is unable to pass back through the glass and becomes trapped, raising the internal temperature.

Space Blankets: Runners use shiny, silvery blankets to stay warm after a race. These blankets reflect body heat back to the runner and are poor emitters of infrared radiation, drastically reducing thermal energy loss.

Solar Water Heaters: These are painted black because dull black surfaces are the best absorbers of infrared radiation from the Sun.

Multiple Methods of Transfer

Many systems involve all three methods simultaneously:

Cooking on a Wood Fire: Thermal energy is transferred through conduction (through the pot), convection (in the air and the water), and radiation (directly from the flames).

Vacuum Flasks: Designed to minimize all heat transfer. A vacuum prevents conduction and convection; silvered surfaces reflect radiation; and a plastic stopper acts as a thermal insulator.
