## Core idea

Just as a magnetic field exists around a magnet, an electric field exists around every electric charge. It is an invisible region where other charges will experience a force. By understanding electric field patterns, physicists can predict how charged particles will move in devices like cathode-ray tubes or particle accelerators.

## Key definitions

- **Electric Field**: A region of space in which an electric charge experiences a force.
- **Direction of Electric Field**: The direction of the field at any point is defined as the direction of the force on a positive charge at that point.
- **Uniform Field**: A field where the electric field lines are parallel and equally spaced, indicating the field strength is the same at all points.
Electric Field Patterns
Electric fields are visualised using field lines with arrowheads that indicate the direction of the force on a positive "test" charge.
Point Charges
- **Positive Point Charge (+Q)**: The field lines are directed radially away from the charge.
- **Negative Point Charge (–Q)**: The field lines are directed radially towards the charge.
Charged Conducting Spheres
- **External Field**: Outside the sphere, the field pattern is exactly the same as that of a point charge located at the sphere's centre.
- **Internal Field**: There are no electric field lines inside a charged conducting sphere.
Parallel Conducting Plates
When two parallel plates are oppositely charged, the field lines start from the positive plate and end on the negative plate.
Near the middle of the plates, the field lines are parallel, indicating a uniform electric field.

- **Electric Current ($I$)**: The charge passing a point per unit time.
- **Ampere (A)**: The SI unit of electric current. One ampere is produced when one coulomb of charge passes a point in a conductor in one second.
- **Coulomb (C)**: The unit of measurement for electric charge ($Q$).
- **Ammeter**: An instrument used to measure the magnitude and direction of an electric current in a circuit.
Key formula
- **Calculating Current**: $$
I = \frac{Q}{t}
$$ Where $Iis the current in amperes (A),Qis the charge in coulombs (C), andt$ is the time in seconds (s).
Conventional Current vs. Electron Flow
Historically, there has been a distinction in how the direction of current is described: - **Conventional Current**: This flows from the positive terminal to the negative terminal. This concept was established before electrons were discovered and is still the standard used in physics textbooks unless otherwise stated.
- **Electron Flow**: This is the actual physical movement of electrons, which flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal because they are repelled by the negative and attracted to the positive.
Electrical Conduction in Metals
Metals are effective electrical conductors because their atoms have free electrons that can detach and move about within the material. When a metal is connected to an energy source (like a battery), these free electrons are driven around the conducting path to form a current.
Measuring Electric Current
To measure current accurately using an ammeter, specific procedures must be followed: - **Series Connection**: The ammeter must be connected in series with the component being measured.
- **Polarity**: Conventional current must enter through the positive (red) terminal and leave through the negative (black) terminal.
- **Range Selection**: It is critical to select the correct range; if the current is too large for the setting, the meter can be damaged, and if it is too small, the reading will be inaccurate.
- **Display Types**: Ammeters can be analogue (using a moving needle) or digital (providing a numerical readout).
Direct Current (d.c.) and Alternating Current (a.c.)
- **Direct Current (d.c.)**: Current that flows in a single direction only. It is typically supplied by cells, batteries, and solar panels.
- **Alternating Current (a.c.)**: Current that changes direction frequently. This is the type of current provided by most mains electricity supplies around the world.

## Steps / method

How to interpret or draw an electric field diagram:

Identify the Source: Determine if the source is positive or negative.

Determine Direction: Draw lines pointing away from positive sources and towards negative sources.

Show Strength: Draw lines closer together where the electric force is stronger (near the charge) and further apart where it is weaker.

Check for Uniformity: If drawing parallel plates, ensure the lines in the centre are straight, parallel, and evenly spaced.

## Common mistakes

Direction Error: Drawing field lines pointing toward a positive charge. Always remember they represent the path a positive charge would take (it would be repelled by another positive charge).

Crossing Lines: Electric field lines never cross each other.

Internal Fields: Drawing field lines inside a solid conducting sphere. The field only exists on the outside.

## Worked example — — Calculating Charge

- **Question**: A lamp has a current of $0.2 \text{ A} flowing through it$. If it is switched on for two hours, what is the total electric charge that passes through the lamp? Step 1: Convert time to seconds.$2 \text{ hours} = 2 \times 60 \times 60 = 7200 \text{ s}$. Step 2: Rearrange $I = Q / t$ to find charge: $Q = I \times t$. Step 3: Substitute the values.Q = 0.2 \text{ A} \times 7200 \text{ s}. Solution: $1440 \text{ C}$ (or $1.4 \times 10^$3 \text{ C}$)$.
