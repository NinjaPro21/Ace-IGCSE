## Core idea

Magnetic materials such as iron and steel differ significantly in how they acquire and retain magnetism. These differences determine whether a material is better suited for a permanent magnet or a temporary one, such as an electromagnet.

Magnetic Softness vs. Hardness

Magnetically Soft (e.g., Soft Iron): These materials are easily magnetised but also lose their magnetism easily. They are used to create temporary magnets.

Magnetically Hard (e.g., Steel): These materials are difficult to magnetise, but once they are magnetised, they retain their magnetism for a long time. They are used to create permanent magnets.

Uses of Magnets

The choice between a temporary or permanent magnet depends on whether a constant or changing magnetic force is required.

Uses of Permanent Magnets

Permanent magnets are used when a constant magnetic force is required.

Magnetic Door Catches: Refrigerator doors use permanent magnetic strips to attract the steel frame and keep the door tightly closed.

Loudspeakers: Use a strong permanent magnet to provide a constant magnetic field for a moving coil.

Uses of Temporary Magnets (Electromagnets)

Electromagnets are temporary magnets used when a changing magnetic force is needed.

Maglev Trains: These trains use both magnetic attraction and repulsion to levitate above the tracks and move forward.

Magnetic Relays: These use armatures made of magnetically soft material (like iron) that are only attracted when a current flows through a coil.

Scrapyard Cranes: Large electromagnets are used to pick up and drop heavy magnetic scrap metal by switching the electric current on and off.
