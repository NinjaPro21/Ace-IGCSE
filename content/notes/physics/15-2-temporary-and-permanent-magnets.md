## Core idea

Magnetic materials such as iron and steel differ significantly in how they acquire and retain magnetism. These differences determine whether a material is better suited for a permanent magnet or a temporary one, such as an electromagnet.

## Key definitions

- **Magnetically soft material**: Easily magnetised but loses magnetism quickly (e.g. soft iron) — used for temporary magnets.
- **Magnetically hard material**: Difficult to magnetise but retains magnetism for a long time (e.g. steel) — used for permanent magnets.
- **Electromagnet**: A temporary magnet created by passing current through a coil around a soft iron core.

## Soft iron vs. steel

| Property | Soft iron (temporary) | Steel (permanent) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ease of magnetisation | Easily magnetised | Difficult to magnetise |
| Strength of induction | Stronger induced magnet | Weaker induced magnet |
| Retention | Loses magnetism easily | Retains magnetism after force removed |

## Uses of permanent magnets

Used when a **constant** magnetic force is needed:

- Refrigerator door catches
- Loudspeaker permanent magnets

## Uses of temporary magnets (electromagnets)

Used when a **switchable** magnetic force is needed:

- Maglev trains
- Magnetic relays
- Scrapyard cranes (pick up and drop metal by switching current)
