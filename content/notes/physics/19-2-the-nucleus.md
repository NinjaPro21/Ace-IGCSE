## Core idea

Deep inside every atom lies a tiny, dense core known as the nucleus, containing nearly all of the atom's mass. Understanding the nucleus is the key to unlocking the secrets of isotopes, which are used in everything from medical imaging to carbon dating. By mastering nuclide notation and the relationship between proton and nucleon numbers, you will gain the fundamental tools needed to navigate the complex world of nuclear physics and radioactivity.

## Key definitions

- **Proton**: A positively charged subatomic particle found in the nucleus. (Relative charge: $+1$; SI unit of charge: coulomb, C).
- **Neutron**: An uncharged (neutral) subatomic particle found in the nucleus. (Relative charge: $0$; SI unit of charge: coulomb, C).
- **Nucleon**: A general term for either a proton or a neutron found within the nucleus.
- **Proton number ($Z$)**: Also called the atomic number, this is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.
- **Nucleon number ($A$)**: Also called the mass number, this is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.
- **Isotopes**: Atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons in their nuclei.
- **Nuclide**: A specific variety of an atomic nucleus characterized by its proton and nucleon numbers.

## Key formulas

**Number of Neutrons**

$Number\ of\ neutrons = A - Z$

This applies to any nuclide to find the count of neutral particles in the core.

**Relative Charge on a Nucleus**

$Relative\ charge = +Z$

The relative charge of the nucleus is equal to the proton number because each proton has a relative charge of $+1$ and neutrons have no charge.

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Simplified Atomic Structure**: A central nucleus containing protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral) is surrounded by negatively charged electrons in orbit.
- **Nuclide Notation**: Represented as ${}^A_Z X$, where $A$ is the nucleon number at the top left, $Z$ is the proton number at the bottom left, and $X$ is the chemical symbol.
- **Isotope Comparison**: Diagrams of isotopes (e.g., Carbon-12 vs. Carbon-14) show the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons in the nucleus.

## Steps / method

Exam Method for Determining Subatomic Particles:

Identify the notation: Look for the ${}^A_Z X$ format provided in the question.

State Protons: The bottom number ($Z$) identifies the number of protons.

Determine Electrons: For a neutral atom, the number of electrons is equal to the proton number $Z$.

Calculate Neutrons: Subtract the proton number from the nucleon number ($A - Z$).

Confirm Isotopes: Check if $Z$ is identical but $A$ is different when comparing two atoms.

## Common mistakes

Electrons in the Nucleus: Thinking electrons are part of the nucleon number; the nucleus contains only protons and neutrons.

Swapping A and Z: Confusing the mass number (top) with the atomic number (bottom) during calculations.

Isotope Identification: Believing that different numbers of neutrons make it a different element; only a change in proton number $Z$ changes the element.

## Examiner tip

Neutrality Check: Always check if the question specifies a "neutral atom." In neutral atoms, the number of electrons equals the number of protons ($Z$).

Chemical vs. Physical: Remember that isotopes have identical chemical properties because they have the same number of protons and electrons, but different physical properties due to different masses.

## Worked example — Basic Nucleus Composition

Question: An atom of Iodine is represented by the nuclide notation ${}^{131}_{53} I. Determine the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus$.

Identify the proton number $Z$. So $Z = 53$ protons.

Identify the nucleon number $A$. So $A = 131$.

Apply formula: Number\ of\ neutrons = 131 - 53 = 78. Final Answer: 53 protons and 78 neutrons.

## Worked example — Identifying Isotopes

Question: Atom X has 17 protons and 18 neutrons. Atom Y has 17 protons and 20 neutrons. Provide the nuclide notation for X and explain the relationship between X and Y.

For Atom X: Z = 17.A = 17 + 18 = 35. Notation is ${}^{35}_{17} X$.

Comparison: Both have 17 protons, making them the same element.

Relationship: They have different neutron counts (18 vs. 20), so they are isotopes. ### Trap

Students often calculate the total nucleons correctly but forget that the proton number defines the element; identical $Z$ values always mean they are isotopes of the same element, even if the mass is different.
