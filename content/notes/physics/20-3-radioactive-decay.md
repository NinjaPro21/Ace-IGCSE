## Core idea

Atoms are not always stable. Some nuclei are like "ticking time bombs" that suddenly change to reach a more stable state. This process, called radioactive decay, is a fundamental part of the natural world. It is responsible for everything from the heat in the Earth’s core to the medical tracers used to save lives in hospitals. By understanding the rules of decay, you can predict exactly how an element will transform when it releases its hidden energy.

## Key definitions

- **Radioactive decay**: A change in an unstable nucleus that can result in the emission of alpha ($\alpha$) particles, beta ($\beta$) particles, and/or gamma ($\gamma$) radiation.
- **Spontaneous**: A process that happens suddenly and cannot be triggered or changed by external conditions like heating, cooling, or chemical means.
- **Random**: A process where it is impossible to predict exactly when a specific nucleus will decay or in which direction the radiation will leave.
- **Alpha ($\alpha$) decay**: A type of decay where the nucleus ejections two protons and two neutrons.
- **Beta ($\beta$) decay**: A type of decay where a neutron in the nucleus changes into a proton and an electron; the proton remains in the nucleus, and the electron is ejected.
- **Gamma ($\gamma$) emission**: The release of excess energy from a nucleus without changing the number of protons or neutrons.

## Key formulas

**Alpha ($\alpha$) decay**

$^{A}{Z}X \rightarrow ^{A-4}{Z-2}Y + ^{4}_{2}\alpha$

Applies when a massive nucleus ejections a helium nucleus to become more stable.

**Beta ($\beta$) decay**

$^{A}{Z}X \rightarrow ^{A}{Z+1}Y + ^{0}_{-1}\beta$

Applies when a nucleus has too many neutrons; the proton number increases by 1.

**Gamma ($\gamma$) emission**

$^{A}{Z}X^* \rightarrow ^{A}{Z}X + \gamma$

Applies when a nucleus is in an "excited" state (shown by asterisk ) and needs to release excess energy.*

## Graphs & diagrams

- **Alpha Decay Visual**: The nucleus loses four nucleons. On a chart of nuclides, the element moves two places back in the periodic table because the proton number decreases by 2.
- **Beta Decay Visual**: The nucleon number ($A$) stays the same, but the proton number ($Z$) increases. This means the element moves one place forward in the periodic table.
- **Decay Equations**: These are written similarly to chemical equations, but they represent nuclear changes rather than electron sharing. The arrow always points from the unstable "parent" nucleus to the more stable "daughter" products.

## Steps / method

Exam Method for Balancing Decay Equations:

Identify the emission: Determine if the question states alpha, beta, or gamma. Balance the Top Numbers ($A$): Ensure the sum of nucleon numbers on the right side of the arrow equals the nucleon number on the left. Balance the Bottom Numbers ($Z$): Ensure the sum of the proton numbers (charges) on the right side equals the proton number on the left.

Identify the new element: Use the new proton number ($Z$) to determine the chemical symbol of the product.

## Common mistakes

Subtracting for Beta: Decreasing the proton number during beta decay instead of increasing it. Remember: beta emission involves losing a negative charge, so the nucleus becomes more positive.

Gamma Changes: Changing $A$ or $Z$ during gamma emission. Gamma is pure energy and has no mass or charge.

External Influence: Suggesting that heating or a chemical reaction can speed up decay. Radioactive decay is completely spontaneous and random.

## Examiner tip

Keywords: When describing decay, always use the words "spontaneous" and "random" to earn marks.

Proton defines the element: If $Z$ changes, the element must change. Always use the resulting $Z$ to check your new element.

Identify the Nature: If asked for the nature of beta radiation, state it is a fast-moving electron.

## Worked example — Alpha Decay

Question: Radon-22$^2$ ($^{222}_{86}Rn$. $Po. ^{4}_{2}\alpha$.

Balance $A$1222 - 4 = 218. The new nucleon number is 218. Balance 2:$86 - 2 = 84. The new proton number is 84$.

Final Equation: $^{222}{86}Rn \rightarrow ^{218}{84}Po + ^{4}_{2}\alpha$.

## Worked example — Multi-step Decay

Question: Lead-214 ($^{214}{82}Pb$. $^{214}{83}Bi^*$), which then emits gamma radiation. Show the combined equation.

Beta Step: The nucleon number 214 remains the same. The proton number 8$^2$ increases to 83. The beta particle is $^{0}_{-1}\beta$.

Gamma Step: The Bismuth nucleus releases energy but the numbers 214 and 83 do not change.

Combined Equation: 
^{214}{82}Pb \rightarrow ^{214}{83}Bi + ^{0}_{-1}\beta + \gamma
