## Core idea

Whether you are warming your hands by a campfire or feeling the chill of an ice cube, you are experiencing the movement of thermal energy. In Physics, heat is constantly in transit. Understanding the rules governing this transfer — specifically that energy always moves from hot to cold — is essential for explaining everything from how emperor penguins survive Antarctic winters to the climate systems of our planet.

## Key definitions

- **Thermal energy**: Energy transferred from one point to another due to a temperature difference.
- **Thermal equilibrium**: Two objects at the same temperature — no net transfer of thermal energy between them.
- **Conduction**: Transfer of thermal energy through a medium (typically solids) without bulk movement of the material.
- **Convection**: Transfer of thermal energy in a fluid (liquid or gas) by convection currents caused by density differences.
- **Radiation**: Transfer of thermal energy as invisible infrared waves; can travel through a vacuum.

## General principles

- **Direction of flow**: Thermal energy always flows from a region of **higher** temperature to a region of **lower** temperature.
- **Requirement for flow**: A net flow occurs only when there is a temperature difference.
- **Sensation of temperature**: An object feels "warm" if thermal energy flows **into** your hand (object is hotter than your skin). It feels "cold" if energy flows **out** of your hand (object is cooler than your skin).
- **Thermal equilibrium**: If your hand and water are both at $37^\circ\text{C}$, you feel neither hot nor cold — no net energy transfer occurs.

## The three methods of transfer

| Method | Where it occurs | How it works |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| **Conduction** | Mainly solids | Particles vibrate or free electrons carry energy to neighbours |
| **Convection** | Fluids only | Warm, less dense fluid rises; cool fluid sinks — bulk movement |
| **Radiation** | Any medium or vacuum | Infrared waves carry energy; no particles needed |

## Steps / method

**Identifying the method of transfer:**

1. **Check the medium**: Is it a solid, fluid, or vacuum/space?
2. **Conduction**: Solid + no visible movement of material → conduction.
3. **Convection**: Fluid + bulk movement (rising/sinking) → convection.
4. **Radiation**: No contact needed, or energy through vacuum (e.g. Sun to Earth) → radiation.
5. **Real situations**: Often all three occur together (e.g. cooking on a fire).

## Common mistakes

**Cold as a substance**: "Cold" does not flow into you — thermal energy flows **out** of your hand to a colder object.

**Convection in solids**: Convection cannot occur in solids because particles cannot move in bulk.

**Radiation needs air**: Radiation does **not** need a medium — it is the only method that works through a vacuum.

## Examiner tip

Always state the **direction** of energy flow (hot → cold). In "explain why it feels cold" questions, describe energy leaving your body, not "cold entering."

## Worked example — Identifying Transfer Methods

Question: Explain how thermal energy reaches Earth from the Sun.

1. **Identify the gap**: Space between the Sun and Earth is a vacuum — no particles to conduct or convect.

2. **Apply the rule**: Only radiation can travel through a vacuum.

3. **Conclusion**: Energy is transferred as infrared (and other electromagnetic) radiation.

**Final answer:** Thermal energy from the Sun reaches Earth by radiation, as this is the only method that does not require a medium.
