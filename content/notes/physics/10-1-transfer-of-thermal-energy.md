## Core idea

Whether you are warming your hands by a campfire or feeling the chill of an ice cube, you are experiencing the movement of thermal energy. In Physics, heat is constantly in transit. Understanding the rules governing this transfer—specifically that energy always moves from hot to cold—is essential for explaining everything from how emperor penguins survive Antarctic winters to the complex climate systems of our planet.

## Key definitions

- **Thermal Energy**: A form of energy that is transferred from one point to another due to a temperature difference.
- **Thermal Equilibrium**: A state where two objects are at the same temperature, resulting in no net gain or loss of thermal energy between them.
- **Conduction**: The transfer of thermal energy through a medium (typically solids) without the bulk movement of the medium itself.
- **Convection**: The transfer of thermal energy in a fluid (liquid or gas) by means of convection currents caused by density differences.
- **Radiation (Thermal)**: The transfer of thermal energy in the form of invisible infrared waves that can travel through a vacuum.
General Principles
- **Direction of Flow**: Thermal energy always flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature.
- **Requirement for Flow**: A net flow of thermal energy occurs only when there is a difference in temperature between two regions or objects.
- **Sensation of Temperature**: An object feels "warm" if your hand is gaining thermal energy from it (because the object is hotter than your skin). Conversely, an object feels "cold" if your hand is losing thermal energy to it (because the object is cooler than your skin).
Thermal Equilibrium and Sensation
Imagine placing your hand in water that is exactly $37^\circ\text{C}$ (typical human body temperature). You would feel neither hot nor cold because your hand and the water are at thermal equilibrium. Since there is no temperature difference to drive the flow of energy, no net transfer occurs. If the water were $50^\circ\text{C}$, thermal energy would flow from the water to your hand, making it feel warm.
Methods of Transfer
- **Thermal energy can be transferred through three distinct processes**: - **Conduction**: Primarily occurs in solids through lattice vibrations and, in metals, free electron diffusion.
- **Convection**: Occurs only in fluids (liquids and gases) through the bulk movement of the material.
- **Radiation**: Carried by infrared waves; it does not require a medium and is the only way thermal energy can travel through the vacuum of space.
