## Core idea

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in an external magnetic field, it experiences a force — the **motor effect**.

## Fleming's left-hand rule

Hold thumb, forefinger, and second finger at right angles:

- **Thumb**: Force (motion)
- **Forefinger**: Magnetic field (N → S)
- **Second finger**: Conventional current

## What causes the motor effect?

The force comes from interaction between the permanent magnet's field and the field from the current in the wire:

- On one side, fields reinforce → stronger combined field.
- On the other side, fields cancel → weaker field.
- The wire is pushed from the stronger field toward the weaker field.

## Forces between parallel conductors

- **Opposite current directions**: Repel
- **Same current directions**: Attract

## Force on charged particle beams

A moving charged particle in a magnetic field experiences a force.

- **Positive particles**: Deflected as predicted by Fleming's left-hand rule.
- **Negative particles**: Force is opposite (current direction is opposite to motion).
- **Reversing the magnetic field** reverses the deflection.

## Graphs & diagrams

**Catapult field**

- Wire carrying current perpendicular to a uniform field between N and S poles.
- Wire field reinforces the main field on one side and cancels on the other — wire pushed toward the weaker side.

## Quick check

Which hand rule is used to find the direction of the force on a current-carrying wire?

Do two parallel wires carrying current in the same direction attract or repel each other?

True or false: A magnetic field can exert a force on a stationary electric charge. (Answer: false — the charge must be moving.)

What happens to the force direction if both the current and the magnetic field are reversed at the same time? (Answer: unchanged)
