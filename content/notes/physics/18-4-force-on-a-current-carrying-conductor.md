## Core idea

When a conductor carrying an electric current is placed in an external magnetic field, it experiences a physical force. This phenomenon is known as the motor effect.

Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule

This rule is used to determine the direction of the force acting on the conductor. When you hold your left hand with the thumb, forefinger, and second finger at right angles to each other:

Thumb: Points in the direction of the force (motion).

Forefinger: Points in the direction of the magnetic field (North to South).

Second Finger: Points in the direction of the conventional current.

What Causes the Motor Effect?

The force is the result of the interaction between two magnetic fields: the field from the permanent magnets and the field produced by the current in the wire.

Reinforcement: On one side of the wire, the two fields act in the same direction, making the combined magnetic field stronger.

Cancellation: On the other side, the fields act in opposite directions, making the combined field weaker.

Result: The wire is pushed from the stronger field toward the weaker field.

Forces Between Parallel Conductors

When two current-carrying wires are placed parallel to each other, their magnetic fields interact, exerting forces on both wires:

Opposite Directions: Conductors carrying currents in opposite directions repel each other.

Same Direction: Conductors carrying currents in the same direction attract each other.

Force on Charged Particle Beams

Since an electric current is a flow of charge, a beam of charged particles moving through a magnetic field also experiences a force.

Positive Charges: A beam of positive particles (like protons) is deflected in the direction predicted by Fleming’s left-hand rule.

Negative Charges: For negative particles (like electrons), the current direction is opposite to their motion. Therefore, the force is in the opposite direction compared to positive particles.

Reversing Fields: Reversing the magnetic field will reverse the direction of the deflection.

## Quick check

Which hand rule is used to find the direction of the force on a current-carrying wire?

Do two parallel wires carrying current in the same direction attract or repel each other?

True or False: A magnetic field can exert a force on a stationary electric charge. (Answer: False; the charge must be moving to experience a magnetic force)

What happens to the direction of the force on a wire if both the current and the magnetic field are reversed at the same time? (Answer: The direction of the force remains the same)
