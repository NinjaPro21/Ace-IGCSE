## Core idea

A lens is a piece of clear glass or plastic with curved surfaces used to focus or spread light. Lenses are foundational in cameras, projectors, and spectacles. Because the surfaces are curved, parallel rays strike different parts at different angles and refract to either converge or diverge.

## Key definitions

- **Principal axis**: Horizontal line through the optical centre, perpendicular to the lens.
- **Optical centre ($C$)**: Midpoint of the lens on the principal axis; rays through $C$ are not refracted.
- **Principal focus ($F$)**: Point where parallel rays converge (converging lens) or appear to diverge from (diverging lens).
- **Focal length ($f$)**: Distance from $C$ to $F$.
- **Focal plane**: Plane through $F$ perpendicular to the principal axis.
- **Converging lens**: Thicker at the centre; parallel rays converge to $F$.
- **Diverging lens**: Thinner at the centre; parallel rays spread out.

## Determining focal length

Hold a converging lens above paper in sunlight. Adjust distance until a small bright spot forms — that distance is $f$. (The spot can burn paper — take care.)

## Worked example — Lens properties

Question: How does refraction differ at the outer edge vs. the centre of a lens?

At the outer edge the angle of refraction is largest due to curvature. Through the optical centre, no refraction occurs — the ray continues straight.

## Quick check

Is a lens thinner at the centre converging or diverging?

What is the distance between the optical centre and the focal point called?

True or false: Rays through the optical centre are refracted.
