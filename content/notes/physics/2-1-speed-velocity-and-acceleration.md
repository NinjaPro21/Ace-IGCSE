## Core idea

Whether studying the record-breaking sprints of Usain Bolt or the seasonal migration of humpback whales, understanding how objects move is fundamental to physics. Mastering these concepts allows you to calculate how fast something travels and how its motion changes over time, skills that are essential for quantifying the interactions of matter and energy in daily life.

## Key definitions

- **Speed**: The distance travelled per unit time. (SI unit: $m/s$)
- **Velocity**: Speed in a given direction. (SI unit: $m/s$)
- **Acceleration**: The change of velocity per unit time. (SI unit: $m/s^2$)
- **Distance**: The total length covered by a moving object regardless of the direction of motion. (SI unit: $m$)
- **Displacement**: The distance measured in a straight line in a specified direction. (SI unit: $m$)
- **Scalar Quantity**: A physical quantity that has magnitude (size) only, such as speed, distance, or mass.
- **Vector Quantity**: A physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction, such as velocity, displacement, or acceleration.

## Key formulas

**Average Speed**

$v = \frac{\text{total distance travelled}}{\text{total time taken}}$

Applies to a whole journey where speed may vary.

**Average Velocity**

$v_{avg} = \frac{\text{total displacement}}{\text{total time taken}}$

Used when direction is specified.

**Acceleration**

$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t}$

Applies when velocity changes over time, where $v$ and $u$ is initial velocity.

## Graphs & diagrams

**Distance-time graphs**

- **Rest**: A horizontal line indicates the distance does not change over time.
- **Constant speed**: Straight diagonal line — same speed throughout.
- **Acceleration**: A curve with an increasing gradient (becoming steeper).
- **Deceleration**: A curve with a decreasing gradient (becoming flatter).
- **Interpretation**: The gradient of the slope represents the speed.

**Speed-time graphs**

- **Rest**: A horizontal line at zero speed.
- **Constant speed**: A horizontal line at a constant speed value (acceleration is zero).
- **Uniform acceleration**: A straight line with a constant positive gradient.
- **Uniform deceleration**: A straight line with a constant negative gradient.
- **Changing acceleration**: A curved line where the gradient changes over time.
- **Interpretation**: The gradient represents acceleration, and the area under the graph represents the distance travelled.

## Steps / method

Identify scalars vs. vectors: Determine if the quantity required has direction (velocity/displacement) or magnitude only (speed/distance).

Assign a direction: For vector calculations, assign one direction as positive (e.g., right or East is "+").

Convert units: Ensure all values are in SI units, converting $km$ to $m$, and minutes or hours to seconds.

Calculate total values: For average speed, sum the total distance and divide by the total time taken.

Calculate changes: For acceleration, find the change in velocity ($\Delta v$) before dividing by the time interval.

## Common mistakes

Confusing Speed and Velocity: Forgetting that velocity must include a direction, whereas speed is just magnitude.

Distance vs. Displacement: Calculating the total path length when the question asks for displacement (the straight-line distance from start to finish).

Unit Errors: Forgetting to convert minutes to seconds or $km$ to $m$, which are necessary for correct SI calculations.

Graph Misinterpretation: Confusing a horizontal line on a distance-time graph (rest) with a horizontal line on a speed-time graph (constant speed).

## Examiner tip

Significant Figures: Always provide final numerical answers to 2 or 3 significant figures unless told otherwise.

Show Formulae: Mark schemes award marks for stating the correct formula and showing the substitution steps even if the final calculation is wrong.

Units: Always include the correct SI unit ($m/s$, $m/s^2$) to avoid losing marks for incomplete answers.

## Worked example — Average speed of a car

Question: A car travels 6 km in 5 minutes. Calculate its average speed in $m/s$.

Convert distance to metres: $6\ \text{km} = 6 \times 1000 = 6000\ \text{m}$.

Convert time to seconds: $5\ \text{minutes} = 5 \times 60 = 300\ \text{s}$.

Use the formula: $\text{Average speed} = 6000\ \text{m} / 300\ \text{s} = 20\ \text{m/s}$. **Final answer:** $20\ \text{m/s}$.

## Worked example — Distance vs. Displacement

Question: A car travels 5 km due East and then makes a U-turn to travel 7 km along the same road. Calculate the total distance and displacement. 1. (Distance) Sum the total path length: $5\ \text{km} + 7\ \text{km} = 12\ \text{km}$. 2. (Displacement) Assign East as positive 2

$\text{Displacement} = +5\ \text{km} + (-7\ \text{km}) = -2\ \text{km}$.

**Final answer:** Distance = $12\ \text{km}$; displacement = $2\ \text{km}$ West of the starting point. ### Trap

Remember that displacement is a vector; it is the shortest distance and direction from the start point, not the total length of the path.
