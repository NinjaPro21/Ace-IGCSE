## Core idea

Resistance is a measure of how difficult it is for an electric current to pass through a conductor, such as a wire. When the resistance of a component is high, it is more difficult for charges to flow, and the resulting current is reduced.

Key Definition and Formula

The resistance ($R$) of a component is defined as the potential difference ($V$) across it divided by the current ($I$) flowing through it.

Formula: $$R = \frac{V}{I}$

SI Unit: The ohm ($\Omega$). One ohm is the resistance of a component when a potential difference of one volt drives a current of one ampere through it.

Measuring Resistance

We can determine the resistance of a component experimentally using the voltmeter–ammeter method:

Setup: Connect an ammeter in series with the component to measure the current ($I$) and a voltmeter in parallel to measure the potential difference ($V$).

Procedure: Use a variable resistor (rheostat) to vary the current in the circuit and record multiple pairs of $V$ and $I$ readings.

Calculation: For each pair, calculate R = V / I. Alternatively, plot a graph ofVagainstI; for an ohmic conductor, the gradient of the graph represents the resistance.

Ohm’s Law

Ohm’s Law states that the current passing through a metallic conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided that physical conditions like temperature remain constant.

Ohmic Conductors: Materials that obey Ohm’s Law (e.g., metals at a constant temperature). Their $I$–$V$ graph is a straight line passing through the origin.

Non-Ohmic Conductors: Materials where resistance varies as the potential difference or current changes.

Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance of a metallic wire depends on three primary physical factors: Length ($l$): Resistance is directly proportional to length. A longer wire provides more "obstacles" for electrons, increasing resistance.

Cross-sectional Area ($A$): Resistance is inversely proportional to the cross-sectional area (thickness). A thicker wire has more space for electrons to flow, which decreases resistance.

Temperature: For most metals, resistance increases as the temperature increases. Note that NTC thermistors are an exception, as their resistance decreases when temperature rises.
