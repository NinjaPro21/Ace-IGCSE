## Core idea

Unlike light, sound cannot travel through a vacuum — it needs a medium of particles that can vibrate. The speed of sound depends on the state of matter: fastest in solids, slowest in gases.

## Key definitions

- **Medium**: A substance (solid, liquid, or gas) whose particles can vibrate to carry sound.
- **Vacuum**: Empty space with no particles — sound cannot travel through it.

**The bell jar experiment**

- An electric bell inside a sealed jar rings normally when air is present.
- As a vacuum pump removes air, the sound fades until it cannot be heard — even though the striker still moves.
- When air is let back in, the sound returns. This shows sound needs a medium.

**Speed of sound in different media**

- **In general**: $\text{speed in gas} < \text{speed in liquid} < \text{speed in solid}$.

| Medium | Approximate speed (m/s) |
| --- | --- |
| Air | 330–350 (often approximated as 300 in calculations) |
| Water | 1500 |
| Iron | 5000 |
| Granite | 5400 |

**Measuring the speed of sound in air**

1. Two observers stand a known distance $d$ apart in an open field.
2. Observer A fires a starting pistol (flash + sound).
3. Observer B starts a stopwatch on seeing the flash and stops on hearing the sound.
4. Speed: $v = \dfrac{d}{t}$.

Note: Light travels at about $3 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s}$, so the flash is seen almost instantly; the delay is due to sound.

## Worked example — Lightning and thunder

Question: A woman stands $1.00\,\text{km}$ ($1000\,\text{m}$) from a storm and hears thunder $3\,\text{s}$ after seeing lightning. Calculate the speed of sound.

1. Use $v = \frac{d}{t}$.
2. Substitute: $v = \frac{1000}{3} = 333\,\text{m/s}$ (2 s.f.).
