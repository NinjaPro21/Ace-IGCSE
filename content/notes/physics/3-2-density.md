## Core idea

Why does a massive steel ship float while a tiny iron ball sinks to the bottom of the ocean? The answer lies in density. Density describes how much mass is packed into a given space. Mastering this concept allows you to identify unknown substances, predict whether objects will float or sink, and understand how complex machines like submarines can move up and down in the water.

## Key definitions

- **Density ($\rho$)**: The mass of a substance per unit volume. (SI unit: kilogram per cubic metre, $kg/m^3$).
- **Mass ($m$)**: A measure of the quantity of matter in an object. (SI unit: kilogram, $kg$).
- **Volume ($V$)**: The amount of space an object occupies. (SI unit: cubic metre, $m^3$).
- **Average Density**: The total mass of an object divided by its total volume; used for objects made of multiple materials like ships or balloons.
- **Meniscus**: The curve at the surface of a liquid; readings should be taken from the bottom of this curve.

## Key formulas

**Density**

$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$

Applies to any substance (solid, liquid, or gas) to determine its compactness 2

**Density Unit Conversion**

$\text{Value in g/cm}^3 \times 1000 = \text{Value in kg/m}^3$

Used to convert common laboratory measurements to SI units.

**Average Density**

$\text{Average Density} = \frac{\text{Total Mass}}{\text{Total Volume}}$

Applies to composite objects like a ship containing both steel and air.

## Graphs & diagrams

**Floating and sinking**

- An object **floats** if its density is less than the liquid.
- An object **sinks** if its density is greater than the liquid.

**Layered liquids**

- When immiscible liquids are in a container, the liquid with the **lowest density** floats on top.
- The liquid with the **highest density** sinks to the bottom.

## Steps / method

Method for determining the density of an irregularly shaped solid that sinks:

Mass: Measure the mass ($m$) of the solid using an electronic balance.

Initial Volume: Fill a measuring cylinder with enough water to cover the object and record the initial volume ($V_1$).

Submersion: Tie a string to the object and lower it gently into the water until it is totally immersed.

Final Volume: Record the new volume reading ($V_2$).

Calculate Volume: Find the volume of the object using $V = V_2 - V_1$.

Calculate Density: Divide the mass by the volume ($\rho = m/V$).

## Common mistakes

Unit Mismatch: Mixing $g$ with $m^3. Always use $g$ with $\text{cm}^3$ or $kg$ with $m^3.

Ignoring Container Mass: Forgetting to subtract the mass of the beaker or cylinder when measuring liquids.

Parallax Error: Reading the volume from an angle rather than level with the meniscus.

Air Bubbles: Leaving air bubbles trapped on an object during the displacement method, which artificially increases the measured volume.

## Examiner tip

Significant Figures: Always round your final density calculation to 2 or 3 significant figures.

Unit Conversions: Remember that to get from $g/cm^3$ to the SI unit $kg/m^3$, you must multiply by 1000.

Precise Reading: State clearly in practical questions that you read the volume at the bottom of the meniscus with your eyes at the same level.

## Worked example — Density of a Liquid

Question: The mass of an empty measuring cylinder is $60.0\ \text{g}$. When $30\ \text{cm}^3$ of olive oil is poured into it, the total mass is $87.6\ \text{g}$. Calculate the density of the olive oil in $\text{g/cm}^3$.

1. Find the mass of the oil: $87.6\ \text{g} - 60.0\ \text{g} = 27.6\ \text{g}$.

2. Identify the volume: $V = 30\ \text{cm}^3$.

3. Apply the formula: 
$\rho = \frac{27.6\ \text{g}}{30\ \text{cm}^3} = 0.92\ \text{g/cm}^3$
.

**Final answer:** The density of the olive oil is $0.92\ \text{g/cm}^3$.

## Worked example — Floating and Sinking (Multi-step)

Question: A piece of gold has a mass of $10.0\ \text{g}$ and a density of $19.3\ \text{g/cm}^3$. If it is placed in a beaker of mercury (density $13.6\ \text{g/cm}^3$), will it float or sink?

1. Compare the densities. The density of gold ($19.3\ \text{g/cm}^3$) is greater than the density of mercury ($13.6\ \text{g/cm}^3$).

2. Apply the floating rule. An object sinks if its density is higher than the liquid it is placed in.

3. **Final answer:** The gold will sink.

### Common Trap

Students often think because mercury is a "heavy" liquid metal, everything floats on it. However, because gold is denser than mercury, it must sink.
