## Core idea

A direct current (d.c.) motor is a device used to convert electrical energy into mechanical energy. It is a fundamental application of the turning effect experienced by a current-carrying coil placed within a magnetic field.

Structure of a D.c. Motor

A simple d.c. motor consists of the following key components:

Armature: A rectangular wire coil (ABCD) mounted on an axle that allows it to rotate.

Permanent Magnets: Positioned on either side of the coil to provide a constant magnetic field.

Split-ring Commutator: A device connected to the ends of the coil that rotates with it.

Carbon Brushes: These press lightly against the commutator to maintain electrical contact with the external circuit.

Rheostat: Often included in the circuit to control the amount of current flowing into the motor.

Principle of Operation

The motor functions through the interaction of magnetic fields:

Force Generation: When current passes through the rectangular coil, the two sides of the coil perpendicular to the magnetic field experience forces in opposite directions. According to Fleming’s left-hand rule, one side experiences a downward force while the other experiences an upward force.

The Turning Effect: These opposing forces create a turning effect (moment) that causes the coil to rotate.

The Role of the Split-ring Commutator: As the coil reaches a vertical position, the commutator reverses the direction of the current in the coil every half a revolution. This reversal ensures that the forces acting on the sides of the coil always drive it in the same direction, allowing for continuous rotation.

Momentum: When the coil is perfectly vertical, the brushes are not in contact with the commutator, and current is momentarily cut off; however, the momentum of the coil carries it past this point to regain contact and continue turning.

Increasing the Turning Effect

To make a d.c. motor more powerful or increase its speed, you can: Insert a soft iron core into the coil to concentrate the magnetic field.

Increase the number of turns in the wire coil.

Increase the current flowing through the coil (e.g., by adjusting the rheostat).

Increase the strength of the permanent magnetic field.

Practical Applications

D.c. motors are widely used in modern technology, including: Battery-operated toys

DVD players

Hard disk drives

Drones (unmanned aerial vehicles), where they provide the necessary thrust for lift-off.
