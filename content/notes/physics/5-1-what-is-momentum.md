## Core idea

An object has momentum whenever it is in motion. Whether it is a fast-moving arrow or a massive elephant chasing a hunter, its momentum is determined by its mass and its velocity. Understanding momentum is essential for analyzing what happens during collisions, from the safety features in your car to the impact of a soccer ball hitting a net.

## Key definitions

- **Momentum ($p$)**: The product of an object's mass and its velocity.
- **SI Unit for Momentum**: kilogram metre per second (kg m/s).
- **Vector Quantity**: A physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction.
- **Velocity**: Speed in a specified direction (SI unit: m/s).
- **Mass**: A measure of the quantity of matter in an object at rest relative to the observer (SI unit: kg).

## Key formulas

**Momentum**

$p = mv$

Applies to any object in motion to determine its "quantity of motion".

**Velocity from momentum**

$v = \frac{p}{m}$

Used to find the speed and direction when momentum and mass are known.

**Mass from momentum**

$m = \frac{p}{v}$

Used to calculate the mass of an object given its momentum and velocity.

## Graphs & diagrams

**Momentum as a vector**

- **Direction**: Momentum acts in the same direction as velocity — always show an arrow when drawing $p$.
- **Magnitude**: Larger mass or higher speed gives larger momentum ($p = mv$).
- **Comparison**: A heavy lorry at low speed can have the same momentum as a light car at high speed.

## Steps / method

Method for solving momentum problems:

Identify mass and velocity: Ensure you have both values from the question.

Convert to SI units: Mass must be in kg and velocity in m/s. For example, convert grams to kg by dividing by 1000.

Assign a direction: Since momentum is a vector, you must choose a positive direction (e.g., right is "+"). Movement in the opposite direction must be assigned a negative value.

Calculate: Multiply the mass by the velocity to find the momentum.

State direction: Always include the direction in your final answer (e.g., "kg m/s to the right").

## Common mistakes

Unit errors: Using mass in grams (g) instead of kilograms (kg). Always convert to kg first ($1000\ \text{g} = 1\ \text{kg}$).

Scalar vs. Vector: Treating momentum as a scalar. Remember that momentum has direction; if two objects move toward each other, one has positive momentum and the other has negative momentum.

Velocity Confusion: Using speed instead of velocity. If an object changes direction but keeps the same speed, its momentum has changed because its velocity changed.

## Examiner tip

Showing Units: To calculate momentum in its SI unit (kg m/s), the mass must be in kg and velocity must be in m/s.

Working Marks: Always state the formula $p = mv$ and show your substitution to ensure you get marks even if you make a calculation error.

Direction: If a question provides a diagram with arrows, use "+" and "–" signs to indicate direction in your calculations.

## Worked example — Calculating Momentum

Question: Calculate the momentum of a runner with a mass of 50 kg running at 4 m/s.

1. Identify values: $m = 50\ \text{kg}$, $v = 4\ \text{m/s}$.

2. Use $p = mv$: $p = 50 \times 4 = 200\ \text{kg m/s}$.

**Final answer:** $200\ \text{kg m/s}$ in the direction of motion.

## Worked example — Finding Mass with a Trap

Question: A car travels at 12 m/s and has a momentum of 14 400 kg m/s. Calculate its mass.

1. Identify values: $p = 14\,400\ \text{kg m/s}$, $v = 12\ \text{m/s}$.

2. Rearrange: $m = p/v = \frac{14\,400}{12} = 1200\ \text{kg}$.

**Final answer:** $1200\ \text{kg}$.

### Common Trap

Direction matters! If an object is moving in the opposite direction to your chosen positive path, its velocity (and therefore its momentum) must be written as a negative value.
