## Core idea

In any complete electrical circuit, there must be an energy provider (such as a battery or dry cell) and an energy user (such as a light bulb or resistor). The concepts of electromotive force (e.m.f.) and potential difference (p.d.) describe how energy is supplied to and used by the circuit.

**Electromotive Force (e.m.f.)**

The energy provider supplies the energy required to drive electric charges around the circuit.

Definition: The electrical work done by a source in moving a unit charge around a complete circuit.

Formula: $$
E = \frac{W}{Q}
$$ Where $Eis the e.m.f. in volts (V),Wis the work done (or energy provided) in joules (J), andQ$ is the charge in coulombs (C).

**Potential Difference (p.d.)**

When a charge passes through an electrical component, work is done on that component as electrical energy is converted into other forms (like heat or light).

Definition: The work done by a unit charge passing through a specific component.

Formula: $$V = \frac{W}{Q}$ Where V is the potential difference in volts (V), W is the work done on the component in joules (J), and Q is the charge in coulombs (C)$.

**Key Differences**

While both quantities are measured in volts, they represent different parts of the energy cycle: e.m.f. is the energy provided to the circuit by a source.

p.d. is the energy converted from electrical form to other forms by a circuit component.

Measuring e.m.f. and p.d.

A voltmeter is used to measure both e.m.f. and p.d..

Parallel Connection: The voltmeter must always be connected in parallel with the component or source being measured.

Polarity: The positive (+) terminal of the voltmeter should connect to the positive terminal of the cell, and the negative (–) terminal should connect to the negative terminal of the cell.

Types: Like ammeters, voltmeters can be analogue (using a needle) or digital (displaying numerical values).
