## Core idea

Linear graphs plot straight lines on a coordinate grid to show constant rates of change between two variables. These lines are defined by their gradient ($m$ and $c$), where the line crosses the vertical axis. Use intercept methods to sketch these relationships quickly, locate boundaries, and visualize linear equations.

## Key formulas

$$
\text{Slope-Intercept Form:} \quad y = mx + c

\text{General Linear Form:} \quad ax + by = d

\text{Intercept Coordinates:} \quad \text{y-intercept occurs at } (0, c), \quad \text{x-intercept occurs at } \left(-\frac{c}{m}, 0\right)
$$

## Steps / method

Rearrange any given linear equation into the standard slope-intercept form $y = mx + c$ to identify the gradient and y-intercept directly.

Alternatively, use the intercept method by substituting $x = 0$ to find the y-intercept coordinate point, and then substituting $y = 0$ to find the x-intercept coordinate point.

Plot both intercept points clearly on the coordinate grid.

Draw a single continuous straight line through both points using a ruler, extending the line across the grid boundaries.

## Examiner tip

When asked to draw a straight line graph on Paper 2, do not guess the path using only the intercept point. Calculate at least three distinct coordinate points spanning the width of the grid axes. If your calculated points do not align perfectly with your ruler, it serves as an immediate warning that you made an arithmetic error.

## Quick check

If a linear equation has a gradient of $m = 0$, its graph is a perfectly horizontal line parallel to the x-axis.

## Worked example — Example 2 (Nov 2022 P21 Q9)

Find the gradient and the y-intercept for the straight line defined by the equation $4x + 3y = 12$.

Rearrange the equation to isolate the $y$. $3y = -4x + 12$.

Divide every term in the equation by $3$ to match the standard form $y = mx + c$. So $y = -\frac{4}{3}x + 4$.

Identify the gradient ($m$ and $x. m = -\frac{4}{3}$ (or $-1.33$).

Identify the y-intercept ($c$. So $c = 4$, corresponding to the coordinate point $(0, 4)$.
