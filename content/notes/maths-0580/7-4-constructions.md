## Core idea

Geometric constructions involve drawing highly accurate shapes, angles, and boundary lines using only a pencil, a ruler, and a pair of compasses. This process includes creating perpendicular bisectors, angle bisectors, and scale drawings based on specific sets of dimensional constraints. Use these construction techniques to locate points that satisfy multiple distance conditions or to map out regions (loci) relative to fixed points and lines.

## Key formulas

$$
\text{Perpendicular Bisector Locus:} \quad \{P : d(P, A) = d(P, B)\} \quad (\text{Equal distance from two fixed points})
$$

$$
\text{Angle Bisector Locus Equation:} \quad \{P : d(P, L_1) = d(P, L_2)\} \quad (\text{Equal distance from two boundary lines})
$$

## Steps / method

To construct a perpendicular bisector between two points $A$ and $B$, open your compass to more than half the distance $AB$.

Place the compass point on $A$ and draw an arc above and below the line.

Keeping the same compass width, place the point on $B$ and draw intersecting arcs.

Use a ruler to draw a straight line through the two intersection points of the arcs.

To construct an angle bisector, place the compass point on the vertex of the angle and draw an arc that cuts through both boundary lines.

Move the compass point to each of these new intersection points and draw two arcs that cross each other inside the angle.

Use a ruler to draw a line from the vertex through this crossing point.

Leave all construction arcs clearly visible on your page; do not erase them after drawing your final lines.

## Examiner tip

Never use a protractor to draw or bisect angles when a question explicitly commands you to "use a ruler and compasses only." Examiners check for the presence of authentic compass punctuation marks and construction arcs. If you draw the correct final line but do not show the required construction arcs, you will lose your method marks.

## Quick check

A perpendicular bisector creates a line where every single point is exactly equal in distance from the two original fixed endpoints.

## Worked example — Example 1 (June 2022 P41 Q5b)

On a diagram of a triangular field $ABC$, construct the locus of points equidistant from the sides $AB$ and $AC$.

Identify the geometric requirement: points equidistant from two lines $AB$ and $AC$ lie on the angle bisector of $\angle BAC$.

Place the compass point firmly on vertex $A$. Draw an arc cutting $AB$ at $X$ and $AC$ at $Y$.

Position the compass point on $X$ and draw an arc inside the triangle area.

Maintaining the exact same compass width, move the point to $Y$ and draw a crossing arc at point $Z$.

Use a straight edge ruler to draw a line from vertex $A$ through $Z$, extending it across the triangle.

## Worked example — Example 2 (Nov 2023 P22 Q8)

Two radar stations are separated by a distance of $8\text{ cm}$ on a map. Construct the boundary line that separates the region closer to station $A$ from the region closer to station $B$.

Recognize that the boundary separating two points is their perpendicular bisector.

Set your compass tracking width to roughly $6\text{ cm}$ (which is safely greater than half of the $8\text{ cm}$ separation line).

Place the compass pivot point on station $A$ and draw a large, continuous semicircular arc that sweeps above and below the line connecting the stations.

Move the compass pivot point over to station $B$, keeping the compass width exactly the same, and draw a matching arc that crosses the first arc at two points (one above and one below the line).

Use a ruler to draw a straight line through these two intersection points to establish the final boundary line.
