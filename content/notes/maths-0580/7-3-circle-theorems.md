## Core idea

Circle theorems are geometric rules that define relationships between angles, lines, chords, and tangents within a circle. These theorems allow you to calculate unknown angles and lengths in complex circle diagrams by identifying key geometric configurations. Use these rules to solve multi-step circle geometry problems, analyze cyclic quadrilaterals, and verify tangent line properties.

## Key formulas

$$
\text{Tangent-Radius Orthogonality:} \quad \angle(\text{Tangent}, \text{Radius}) = 90^\circ
$$
$$
\text{Angle at the Center Theorem:} \quad \angle \text{at Center} = 2 \times \angle \text{at Circumference}
$$

$$
\text{Cyclic Quadrilateral Rule:} \quad \angle_{\text{Opposite A}} + \angle_{\text{Opposite B}} = 180^\circ
$$
$$
\text{Alternate Segment Theorem:} \quad \angle(\text{Tangent}, \text{Chord}) = \angle \text{in Alternate Segment}
$$

## Steps / method

Scan the circle diagram to identify key components: radii, diameters, chords, tangents, or inscribed four-sided shapes (cyclic quadrilaterals).

Check for diameter lines first: an angle subtended from a diameter to the circumference is always a right angle ($90^\circ$).

Look for shared arcs or chords: angles subtended from the same arc to the circumference are equal, and the angle subtended at the center is double the angle at the circumference.

For four-sided shapes inscribed in the circle, apply the cyclic quadrilateral rule: ensure opposite angles add up to $180^\circ$.

If a tangent line touches the circle, apply the tangent-radius rule ($90^\circ$) or the alternate segment theorem to find missing angles.

## Examiner tip

When an exam question asks you to find an angle in a circle diagram and states "give reasons for your answer," you must write out the full name of each circle theorem you use. Writing shorthand descriptions like "arrowhead rule" or "cyclic quad" will lose you marks. Use the formal terms, such as "the angle at the center is twice the angle at the circumference."

## Quick check

Angles subtended at the circumference by the same arc or chord are always equal to each other.

## Worked example — Example 1 (June 2022 P22 Q16)

Points $A$, $B$, and $C$ lie on the circumference of a circle with centre $O$. Given that $\angle ABC = 54^\circ$, find $\angle AOC$.

Identify the geometric configuration: $\angle ABC$ is the angle at the circumference standing on arc $AC$.

Recall the relevant circle theorem: the angle subtended at the centre is double the angle subtended at the circumference by the same arc. Set up the calculation: $\angle AOC = 2 \times \angle ABC$.

Substitute the given value to find the answer: $\angle AOC = 2 \times 54^\circ = 108^\circ$.

## Worked example — Example 2 (Nov 2023 P41 Q4b)

$ABCD$ is a cyclic quadrilateral inscribed in a circle.

Given that $\angle BAC = 42^\circ$ and $\angle ADC = 78^\circ$, find $\angle ACB$.

Identify that $ABCD$ is cyclic, so opposite angles sum to $180^\circ$: $\angle ABC + \angle ADC = 180^\circ$.

Calculate angle $\angle ABC$: $\angle ABC = 180^\circ - 78^\circ = 102^\circ$.

Look at triangle $ABC$: the angles in a triangle sum to $180^\circ$, so $\angle BAC + \angle ABC + \angle ACB = 180^\circ$.

Substitute the known values into the triangle equation: $42^\circ + 102^\circ + \angle ACB = 180^\circ$.

Simplify and solve for the missing angle: $144^\circ + \angle ACB = 180^\circ \implies \angle ACB = 180^\circ - 144^\circ = 36^\circ$.
