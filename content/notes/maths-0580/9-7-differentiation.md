## Core idea

Differentiation finds $\frac{dy}{dx}$, the gradient at a point. Set $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$ to locate turning points.

## Key formulas

**Power rule**

$$
y = ax^n \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dx} = nax^{n-1}
$$

**Linear function**

$$
y = mx + c \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dx} = m
$$

**Turning point**

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = 0
$$

## Steps / method

Write each term in index form (e.g. $\frac{2}{x} = 2x^{-1}$).

Differentiate term by term with the power rule.

Constants differentiate to $0$.

Substitute an $x$ when $2$ for turning points.

## Examiner tip

Convert fractions to negative indices before differentiating — do not differentiate $\frac{2}{x}$ as $\frac{2}{1}$.

## Quick check

At a turning point the tangent is horizontal, so $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$.

## Worked example — Example 1 (June 2023 P42 Q9a)

Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ if $y = 2x^3 - 5x^2 + 4x - 7$.

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = 6x^2 - 10x + 4
$$

## Worked example — Example 2 (Nov 2022 P41 Q10c)

Find the turning point of $y = x^2 - 6x + 5$.

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 3
$$

$$
y = 3^2 - 6(3) + 5 = -4 \Rightarrow (3,-4)
$$
