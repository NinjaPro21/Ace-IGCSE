## Core idea

Symmetry analyzes how geometric shapes remain invariant under reflection or rotation. Line symmetry (reflectional) divides a 2D shape into mirror-image halves along a line of symmetry, while rotational symmetry measures how many times a shape matches its original outline during a full $360^\circ$ rotation. Use these symmetry rules to classify shapes, analyze 3D solids, and solve design problems.

## Key formulas

$$
\text{Order of Rotational Symmetry:} \quad \text{Number of outline matches during a full } 360^\circ \text{ rotation}

\text{Regular Polygon Symmetry Rule:} \quad \text{Number of Lines of Symmetry} = \text{Order of Rotational Symmetry} = n \text{ sides}
$$

## Steps / method

To find lines of symmetry on a 2D shape, look for axes where you could fold the shape exactly in half so that all edges and corners match perfectly.

To determine the order of rotational symmetry, imagine rotating the shape around its center point through a full $360^\circ$ turn, counting how many times it locks back into its original footprint.

For 3D structures, look for flat planes of symmetry that slice the solid into identical mirror-image halves, or axes of rotation through the center.

Record your findings as a single whole integer value representing the total count or order.

## Examiner tip

Be careful not to confuse the rotational symmetry of a regular shape with that of an oblique parallelogram. A standard parallelogram has zero lines of symmetry, but it has an order of rotational symmetry equal to 2 (it fits its footprint twice during a full turn). Drawing imaginary fold lines on a diagram can help you avoid mixing up these properties on Paper 2.

## Quick check

An isosceles triangle has exactly one line of symmetry and an order of rotational symmetry equal to 1.

## Worked example — Example 1 (June 2021 P22 Q2)

State the number of lines of symmetry and the order of rotational symmetry for a standard regular hexagon.

Identify the number of sides on a regular hexagon: $n = 6$.

Apply the regular polygon symmetry rule: for any regular polygon, the number of lines of symmetry matches its number of sides. This gives $6$ lines of symmetry.

Apply the rotational symmetry rule for regular shapes: the order of rotational symmetry al so matches its number of sides. This gives an order of rotational symmetry equal to $6$.
