## Core idea

Graphical solutions involve finding the roots or solving systems of equations by locating where their graphs intersect on a coordinate grid. The x-coordinates of the intersection points between two functions, $y = f(x)$ and $y = g(x)$, represent the exact solutions to the combined equation $f(x) = g(x)$. Use this method to estimate solutions for complex polynomial equations that are difficult to solve analytically.

## Key formulas

$$
\text{Intersection Equation Equivalence:} \quad y = f(x) \quad \text{and} \quad y = g(x) \implies f(x) = g(x)

\text{Root Solution Extraction Set:} \quad \text{Roots occur at } x_k \quad \text{where } y_{\text{curve}} = 0 \quad (\text{x-axis crossings})
$$

## Steps / method

Plot or reference the existing graph of the function $y = f(x)$ on the coordinate grid.

Rearrange the target equation to isolate the primary function on one side, identifying what secondary function line $y = g(x)$ needs to be drawn.

Draw the straight line or curve for $y = g(x)$ across the same coordinate grid.

Locate all points where the two graphs intersect, and drop vertical lines from these intersections to read the solution values directly off the x-axis.

## Examiner tip

When reading graphical solutions off a grid on Paper 4, do not guess or round your answers to the nearest whole integer. Use a ruler to line up the intersection point with the scale on the x-axis, and read the decimal value as accurately as the grid subdivisions allow. Cambridge mark schemes include a strict tolerance range (usually $\pm 0.05$) for these readings.

## Quick check

The solutions to the equation $f(x) = 0$ are the x-coordinates where the graph of $y = f(x)$ crosses the horizontal x-axis.

## Worked example — Example 1 (June 2022 P42 Q4b)

Use your curve graph of $y = x^3 - 3x$. So $x^3 - 3x = 1$.

Set up the intersection relationship: let $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$. So $g(x) = 1$. This means we need to find where the curve intersects the horizontal line $y = 1$.

Draw a straight, horizontal line at $y = 1$ across your coordinate grid using a ruler.

Identify the three points where the curve cuts through this horizontal line.

Read the x-coordinates of these intersection points carefully from the horizontal axis scale:$x_1 \approx -1.53, \quad x_2 \approx -0.35, \quad x_3 \approx 1.88$.

## Worked example — Example 2 (Nov 2023 P41 Q5d)

Given the curve $y = x^2 - 4x$. So $x^2 - 5x - 2 = 0$.

Write down the target equation to solve: $x^2 - 5x - 2 = 0$.

Rearrange the target equation so that the left side matches the existing curve equation ($x^2 - 4x$. So $x^2 - 4x - x - 2 = 0 \implies x^2 - 4x = x + 2$.

Substitute $y$. So $y = x + 2$.

State that drawing the linear straight line $y = x + 2$ on the grid will reveal the solutions at its intersection points with the curve.
