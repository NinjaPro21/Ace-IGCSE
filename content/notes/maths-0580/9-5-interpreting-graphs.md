## Core idea

Graphs turn a story into a picture. In IGCSE you often get **real-life graphs** — especially distance–time and speed–time (velocity–time) for a journey.

**The golden rules:**

- **Distance–time:** gradient = **speed**

- **Speed–time:** gradient = **acceleration**, area under graph = **distance travelled**

## Reading distance–time graphs

| What you see | What it means |
|--------------|---------------|
| Flat horizontal line | Standing still |
| Straight line going up | Constant speed |
| Curved like a **smile ☺** | Speeding up (accelerating) |
| Curved like a **frown ☹** | Slowing down (decelerating) |
| Line going down | Moving back towards the start |

Steeper line = faster. No slope = not moving.

## Reading speed–time graphs

| What you see | What it means |
|--------------|---------------|
| Flat horizontal line | Constant speed (no acceleration) |
| Line sloping up | Accelerating |
| Line sloping down | Decelerating |
| Area under the graph | Total distance |

Split weird shapes into **rectangles** and **triangles**, find each area, add them up.

## Key formulas

$$
\text{Speed from d-t graph:} \quad \text{gradient} = \frac{\Delta \text{distance}}{\Delta \text{time}}
$$
$$
\text{Acceleration from v-t graph:} \quad \text{gradient} = \frac{\Delta \text{speed}}{\Delta \text{time}}
$$
$$
\text{Distance from v-t graph:} \quad \text{area under the curve}
$$

## Steps / method

Read the axis labels — distance–time or speed–time?

For **speed** from d–t: pick a straight section, do rise ÷ run

For **acceleration** from v–t: gradient of the sloping part

For **distance** from v–t: find area (triangle = ½ × base × height, rectangle = base × height)

Check units match (convert minutes to seconds if needed!)

## Worked example — Area under a speed–time graph

A cyclist accelerates from rest to $12 \text{ m/s}$ in $10 \text{ s}$, rides at $12 \text{ m/s}$ for $30 \text{ s}$, then stops in $5 \text{ s}$.

Split the graph into a triangle, a rectangle, and a triangle:

- Phase 1: $\frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 12 = 60 \text{ m}$
- Phase 2: $30 \times 12 = 360 \text{ m}$
- Phase 3: $\frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 12 = 30 \text{ m}$

**Total distance** $= 60 + 360 + 30 = 450 \text{ m}$

## Worked example — Speed from a distance–time graph

A vehicle travels $150 \text{ m}$ in $6 \text{ s}$, stops for $4 \text{ s}$, then travels $200 \text{ m}$ in $5 \text{ s}$. Final leg speed = gradient of last section:

$$
\frac{200}{5} = 40 \text{ m/s}
$$

## Common mistakes

- Using distance–time rules on a speed–time graph (or vice versa)

- Forgetting to convert time units before finding gradient or area

- Thinking a flat d–t graph means "constant speed" — it means **stationary**

## Examiner tip

Always check units on both axes. If time is in minutes and speed in m/s, convert time to seconds first. Label your areas on the graph in the exam — it helps you pick up method marks.
