## Core idea

Inequalities describe ranges of values or regions on a graph where expressions are bound by directional restrictions rather than strict equality. They are solved algebraically using the same steps as linear equations, and represented graphically as regions bounded by solid or dashed lines. Use inequalities to model constraints, define acceptable tolerances, and solve linear programming optimization problems.

## Key methods

**Algebraic solving** — treat like a linear equation, but **flip the inequality sign** when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number.

**Number line** — open circle for $<$ or $>$; closed (filled) circle for $\le$ or $\ge$; arrow shows the direction of values that satisfy the inequality.

**Graphical regions** — draw the boundary line ($y = mx + c$), then shade the unwanted region or mark the wanted region R as instructed.

**Line style:** solid line for $\le$ or $\ge$ (boundary included); dashed line for $<$ or $>$ (boundary excluded).

## Steps / method

Solve algebraic inequalities using the same isolation steps as linear equations.

Remember to flip the inequality direction if you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number.

To graph an inequality boundary, treat it as a linear equation ($y = mx + c$) and draw the line on the grid.

Use a solid line for inclusive inequalities ($\le$, $\ge$) and a dashed line for strict inequalities ($<$, $>$).

Identify the correct side of the boundary line by testing a coordinate point not on the line, such as $(0, 0)$, in the inequality.

Shade the rejected region or mark the accepted region with a letter R as directed by the question instructions.

## Examiner tip

When shading regions on Paper 4, read the instructions carefully to see whether you should shade the wanted region or the unwanted region. The standard Cambridge convention is often to shade the unwanted region to leave the true solution region R clear and unshaded. Misreading this instruction can cost you all your graphing marks.

## Quick check

If you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must reverse the direction of the inequality sign.

## Worked example — Example 1 (June 2022 P22 Q11)

Solve the inequality: $7 - 3x \le 19$.

Isolate the term containing $x$ by subtracting $7$ from both sides: $-3x \le 19 - 7$, so $-3x \le 12$.

Divide both sides by $-3$ to isolate $x$. Since you are dividing by a negative number, you must reverse the direction of the inequality sign: $x \ge \frac{12}{-3}$.

Simplify the division to find the final answer: $x \ge -4$.

## Worked example — Example 2 (Nov 2023 P41 Q6a)

Find the three linear inequalities that define the unshaded region R bounded by a solid horizontal line at $y = 2$, a dashed vertical line at $x = 5$, and a solid line passing through $(0, 0)$ with a gradient of $1$.

Analyze the horizontal boundary line: it is a solid line at $y = 2$. The region R lies above it, so the inequality is $y \ge 2$.

Analyze the vertical boundary line: it is a dashed line at $x = 5$. The region R lies to the left of it, so the inequality is strict: $x < 5$.

Analyze the sloping boundary line: it is a solid line passing through the origin with a gradient of $1$, which gives the equation $y = x$. The region R lies below this line, so the inequality is $y \le x$.

Collect the three inequalities that define the region: $y \ge 2$, $x < 5$, and $y \le x$.
