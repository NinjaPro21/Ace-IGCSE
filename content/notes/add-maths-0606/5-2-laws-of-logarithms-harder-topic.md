## Core idea

The laws of logarithms allow us to combine multiple logarithmic terms into a single expression or expand a single term into several. These laws are direct counterparts to the laws of indices (e.g., multiplying numbers corresponds to adding their logs).

## Key formulas

$\log_a(xy) = \log_a x + \log_a y$

$\log_a\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_a x - \log_a y$

$\log_a(x^n) = n\log_a x$

## Steps / method

Simplify coefficients: Use the Power Law to move any numbers in front of the logs to the exponent position (e.g. $2\log x \to \log x^2$).

Combine terms: Use the Product and Quotient laws to condense the expression into a single log.

Expand: Conversely, use the laws in reverse to break down complex expressions for easier differentiation or solving.

## Worked example — Condensing

Question: Express $2\log_a 3 + \log_a 4 - \log_a 2$ as a single logarithm.

Power Law: $\log_a(3^2) + \log_a 4 - \log_a 2 = \log_a 9 + \log_a 4 - \log_a 2$.

Product Law: $\log_a(9 \times 4) - \log_a 2 = \log_a 36 - \log_a 2$.

Quotient Law: $\log_a\left(\frac{36}{2}\right) = \log_a 18$.

## Worked example — Expanding

Question: Given $\log_b 2 = p$ when $. in terms ofp$ and $q$.

Rewrite as power: $\log_b(12^{1/2}) = \frac{1}{2}\log_b 12$. Factor 12: $\frac{1}{2}\log_b(2^2 \times 3)$.

Expand: $\frac{1}{2}[\log_b(2^2) + \log_b 3] = \frac{1}{2}[2\log_b 2 + \log_b 3]$.

Substitute: $\frac{1}{2}[2p + q] = p + 0.5q$.

## Examiner tip

A common error is thinking $\log(x + y) = \log x + \log y$. This is incorrect. The laws only apply to products, quotients, and powers — not sums or differences inside the log.

## Quick check

$\log_a(x) - \log_a(y)$ simplifies to $\log_a\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)$, which is not the same as $\frac{\log_a x}{\log_a y}$.
