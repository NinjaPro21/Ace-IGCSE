## Core idea

Quadratic inequalities ask for the range of $x$-values where a quadratic curve sits either strictly above ($> 0$) or below ($< 0$) the horizontal x-axis. Solving them requires finding the critical boundary roots first, followed by visual inspections of a sketch to determine the intervals.

## Key formulas

$$
ax^2 + bx + c > 0 \quad \text{or} \quad ax^2 + bx + c < 0

\text{Critical values: } x_1, x_2 \quad (\text{where } x_1 < x_2)
$$


## Steps / method

Rearrange the entire inequality so that all terms are on one side, leaving zero on the opposite side.

Temporarily replace the inequality sign with an equals sign and solve $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ to find critical root boundaries.

Sketch a quick, rough diagram of the parabola based on whether $a$ is positive (smiley) or negative (frowning).

For $f(x) > 0$ (with $a > 0$), choose the outer regions: $x < x_1$ or $x > x_2$.

For $f(x) < 0$ (with $a > 0$), choose the inner bounded region: $x_1 < x < x_2$.

### Key rule

Find critical values from $f(x) = 0$, then use the parabola's shape ($a > 0$ opens up, $a < 0$ opens down) to choose outer or inner intervals.

## Worked example — Strictly above the axis

Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 > 0$.

Factorise: $(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$, so critical values are $x = 2$ and $x = 3$.

Since $a = 1 > 0$, the parabola is above the axis in the outer regions: $x < 2$ or $x > 3$.

## Worked example — Strictly below the axis

Solve $2x^2 + 3x - 2 < 0$.

Factorise: $(2x - 1)(x + 2) = 0$, giving $x = \frac{1}{2}$ and $x = -2$.

Since $a = 2 > 0$ and we need $f(x) < 0$, the solution is the inner interval: $-2 < x < \frac{1}{2}$.

## Examiner tip

Never write two separate outer inequalities as a single joined chain like $5 < x < 2$. Write separate individual inequalities using the word "or" ($x < 2 \text{ or } x > 5$) to maintain logical and mathematical correctness.

## Quick check

If $a > 0$ and the inequality asks for $f(x) < 0$, then the final solution is always a single combined range between the two roots.
