## Core idea

This topic uses a line of best fit from experimental data to determine the original equation. You read the gradient and intercept of the straight-line graph and reverse the linearisation to find the original constants.

## Key formulas

**Gradient from two points**

$$
m = \frac{Y_2 - Y_1}{X_2 - X_1}
$$

**Recovering original constants**

Use the mappings from Topic 6.4 — e.g. if $Y = \lg y$ and $c = \lg a$, then $a = 10^c$.

## Steps / method

1. **Find the gradient** $m$ using two points on the straight-line graph.

2. **Read the intercept** $c$ where the line crosses the vertical axis.

3. **Relate to the original equation** using the $Y$, $X$, $m$, $c$ mappings from 6.4.

### Key rule

Use two points **on the line of best fit** that are far apart — not raw data points that do not lie exactly on the line.

## Worked example — Power law from graph

Question: A graph of $\lg y$ against $\lg x$ has gradient 2 and vertical intercept 0.477. Find the relationship between $y$ and $x$.

$$
\lg y = 2 \lg x + 0.477
$$

Gradient $m = n = 2$. Intercept $c = \lg a = 0.477$, so:

$$
a = 10^{0.477} \approx 3
$$

Final relationship: $y = 3x^2$.

## Examiner tip

Never use table data points that do not lie on your line of best fit. Pick two points far apart on the line itself for maximum accuracy.

## Quick check

If the gradient of a graph of $\lg y$ against $\lg x$ is negative, the original equation has a negative power, such as $y = ax^{-n}$.
