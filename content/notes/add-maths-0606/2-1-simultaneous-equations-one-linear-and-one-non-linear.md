## Core idea

This topic involves finding the coordinates where a straight line and a curve (usually a quadratic or circle) intersect. Because one equation is non-linear, you cannot use simple elimination; you must use **substitution** to create a single quadratic equation.

## Key formulas

**Linear equation**

$$
y = mx + c
$$

**Quadratic equation (standard form)**

$$
ax^2 + bx + c = 0
$$

**Quadratic formula**

$$
x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
$$

## Steps / method

1. **Rearrange** the linear equation to make $x$ or $y$ the subject.
2. **Substitute** into the non-linear equation to eliminate one variable.
3. **Expand and simplify** to standard quadratic form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
4. **Solve** the quadratic (factoring or formula) to find two values.
5. **Substitute back** into the linear equation to find the paired coordinates.

### Key rule

Always substitute your answers back into the **linear** equation to find the second variable.

## Worked example — Line and circle

Question: Solve the simultaneous equations $y = 2x - 1$ and $x^2 + y^2 = 5$.

Substitute $y = 2x - 1$ into the circle equation:

$$
x^2 + (2x - 1)^2 = 5
$$

Expand:

$$
x^2 + 4x^2 - 4x + 1 = 5 \Rightarrow 5x^2 - 4x - 4 = 0
$$

Use the quadratic formula:

$$
x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4(5)(-4)}}{2(5)} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{96}}{10}
$$

Calculate $y = 2x - 1$ for each value of $x$ to get the coordinate pairs.

## Examiner tip

Always substitute your findings back into the linear equation to find the second variable. Substituting into the non-linear equation can sometimes produce extraneous solutions.

## Quick check

If the resulting quadratic has no real roots, the line and curve do not intersect.
