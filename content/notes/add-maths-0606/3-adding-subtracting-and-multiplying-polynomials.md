## Core idea

Polynomial algebraic manipulation forms the foundation for simplifying complex algebraic functions. Use addition and subtraction by combining like terms (matching exponents), and multiplication by systematically distributing every term of one polynomial into another. This process prepares expressions for further algebraic factorization, sketching, or coordinate intersections.

## Key formulas

$$
\deg\bigl[f(x) \pm g(x)\bigr] \le \max(\deg f,\ \deg g)
$$
$\deg\bigl[f(x) \times g(x)\bigr] = \deg f + \deg g$

## Steps / method

To add or subtract polynomials, align matching terms of the same degree (e.g., collect all $x^2$ terms together) and add or subtract their coefficients.

Remember to distribute any negative sign outside a bracket to every single term inside the bracket when subtracting.

To multiply polynomials, multiply each term in the first polynomial by every term in the second polynomial.

Group and sum all resulting coefficients of identical powers to present your polynomial in general descending order ($ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$).

### Key rule

Only like terms (matching powers) can be combined; when multiplying polynomials, the degree of the product equals the sum of the individual degrees.

## Worked example — Multiplying quadratics

Expand $(x^2 + 2x - 3)(x - 1)$.

Multiply each term: $x^3 - x^2 + 2x^2 - 2x - 3x + 3 = x^3 + x^2 - 5x + 3$.

The result is a cubic polynomial of degree 3.

## Worked example — Subtracting with a negative sign

Simplify $(3x^2 - 4x + 1) - (x^2 - 2x + 5)$.

Distribute the negative sign: $3x^2 - 4x + 1 - x^2 + 2x - 5 = 2x^2 - 2x - 4$.

## Examiner tip

A classic mistake occurs when expanding expressions with multiple variables or negative signs. For instance, when expanding expressions such as $-(2x^2-3x+1)$, students frequently forget to change the sign of the inner terms, incorrectly leading to $-2x^2-3x+1$ instead of $-2x^2+3x-1$.

## Quick check

If two polynomials of degree 2 are multiplied together, then the resulting polynomial will always have a maximum degree of 4.
