## Core idea

This section deals with complex problems that blend AP and GP concepts or involve real-world modelling, such as population growth (GP) or monthly loan repayments (AP). Success here depends on identifying which type of sequence applies to each part of the problem.

## Steps / method

1. **Extract the sequence** — write out the first three terms to check if it is an AP or GP.

2. **Break down compound problems** — if a problem switches types halfway, set up equations for both and solve simultaneously.

3. **Real-world context** — for percentage increases (e.g. 5% growth), use $r = 1.05$; for decreases, use $r < 1$.

### Key rule

When AP terms al so form a GP, equate the middle term squared to the product of the first and third GP terms.

## Worked example — AP terms forming a GP

Question: The first, second, and fifth terms of an AP are the first three terms of a GP. If the AP's first term is 3, find the common difference $d$.

AP terms: $3$, $3+d$, $3+4d$

For a GP: $(3+d)^2 = 3(3+4d)$

So $9 + 6d + d^2 = 9 + 12d$, giving $d^2 - 6d = 0$

Hence $d(d - 6) = 0$, so $d = 0$ or $d = 6$

Since $d = 0$ gives a constant sequence, the non-trivial answer is $d = 6$.

## Examiner tip

In real-life questions like banking or population, check whether $n$ represents the end of a time period or the start. This determines whether you need $u_n$ or $u_{n+1}$.

## Quick check

If the terms of a series change from positive to negative and back, it is likely a GP with a negative common ratio.
