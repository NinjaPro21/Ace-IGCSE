## Core idea

An **arithmetic progression** (AP) has a constant difference $d$ between consecutive terms. APs model linear growth or decline, such as regular savings or simple patterns.

## Key formulas

**$n$th term**

$$
u_n = a + (n-1)d
$$

**Sum of first $n$ terms**

$$
S_n = \frac{n}{2}\bigl[2a + (n-1)d\bigr] = \frac{n}{2}(a + l)
$$

where $a$ is the first term, $d$ is the common difference, and $l$ is the last term.

## Steps / method

1. **Identify** $a$ and $d$ — find $d$ by subtracting consecutive terms.
2. **Substitute** into $u_n$ or $S_n$ as required.
3. **Solve** for $n$ — remember $n$ must be a positive integer.

### Key rule

If the sequence decreases (e.g. $20, 15, 10, \ldots$), then $d$ is negative.

## Worked example — Finding which term

Question: An AP starts $5, 8, 11, \ldots$. Which term equals $152$?

$a = 5$, $d = 3$. Use $u_n = 152$:

$152 = 5 + (n-1)(3)$, so $147 = 3(n-1)$, giving $n - 1 = 49$ and $n = 50$.

## Worked example — Finding $a$ and $d$

Question: In an AP, the 3rd term is $10$ and the 8th term is $30$. Find $a$ and $d$.

$u_3$: $a + 2d = 10$. $u_8$: $a + 7d = 30$.

Subtract: $5d = 20$, so $d = 4$. Substitute: $a + 8 = 10$, so $a = 2$.

## Examiner tip

Watch for negative common differences. If terms are decreasing, $d < 0$ — forgetting the sign is a common error in the sum formula.

## Quick check

A sequence is an AP only if the difference between consecutive terms is constant.
