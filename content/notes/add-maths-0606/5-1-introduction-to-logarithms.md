## Core idea

Logarithms are the inverse operation of exponentiation. If $a^x = y$, then $x = \log_a y$. A logarithm tells you the **power** you must raise the base to in order to get a given number.

## Key formulas

**Exponential ↔ logarithmic form**

$$
a^x = y \iff x = \log_a y
$$

**Special values**

$$
\log_a a = 1,
$$

$$
\log_a 1 = 0
$$

## Steps / method

1. **Identify the parts** — recognise the base $a$, the exponent $x$, and the result $y$.

2. **Rearrange** — swap between exponential and logarithmic form to isolate the unknown.

3. **Evaluate** — use a calculator (base 10 or $e$) or mental math for simple powers.

### Key rule

The base must satisfy $a > 0$ and $a \neq 1$. You cannot take the logarithm of zero or a negative number.

## Worked example — Converting and evaluating

Question: Write $10^{3} = 1000$ in logarithmic form and evaluate $\log_2 16$.

Exponential to log: the base is 10 and the exponent is 3, so:

$$
\log_{10} 1000 = 3
$$

Evaluate $\log_2 16$: ask "2 to what power gives 16?"

$$
2^4 = 16 \Rightarrow \log_2 16 = 4
$$

## Examiner tip

The base of a logarithm must always be positive ($a > 0$) and not equal to 1. If an algebraic solution gives $\log(\text{negative})$, reject that solution.

## Quick check

If $\log_b x = y$, then $x = b^y$.
