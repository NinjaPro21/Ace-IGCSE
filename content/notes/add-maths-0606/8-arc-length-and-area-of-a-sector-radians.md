## Core idea

Circular measure uses radians—where an angle is measured by the length of the arc it subtends on a unit circle—to simplify geometric calculations. When angles are measured in radians rather than degrees, the formulas for arc length and sector area scale linearly with the angle, making conversion factors unnecessary.

## Key formulas

$$
\theta_{\text{radians}} = \theta_{\text{degrees}} \times \frac{\pi}{180^\circ}
$$
$$
\text{Arc Length: } s = r\theta \quad (\text{where } \theta \text{ is in radians})
$$
$$
\text{Area of Sector: } A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta \quad (\text{where } \theta \text{ is in radians})
$$

## Steps / method

Check the units of the angle given in the problem. If it is in degrees, convert it to radians by multiplying by $\frac{\pi}{180}$.

To find the arc length along the outer crust of the sector, multiply the radius by the central angle: $s = r\theta$.

To find the total area of the pizza-shaped sector, square the radius, multiply by the angle, and halve the result: $A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$.

To find the area of a segment, subtract the area of the central triangle ($\frac{1}{2}r^2\sin\theta$) from the total sector area.

### Key rule

Radians only: $s = r\theta$ and $A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$. Convert degrees with $\theta_{\text{rad}} = \theta_{\text{deg}} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$.

## Worked example — Arc length

A sector has radius $6$ cm and central angle $\frac{\pi}{3}$ radians. Find the arc length.

$$
s = r\theta = 6 \times \frac{\pi}{3} = 2\pi \text{ cm}
$$

## Worked example — Sector area

Find the area of a sector with radius $10$ cm and angle $1.2$ radians.

$$
A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(10)^2(1.2) = 60 \text{ cm}^2
$$

## Examiner tip

Always make sure your calculator is switched to Radian (RAD) mode when evaluating trigonometric functions (like $\sin\theta$ for a triangle area) in circular measure questions. Leaving your calculator in Degree (DEG) mode will lead to incorrect answers.

## Quick check

If a sector has a central angle of exactly $\theta = 2$ radians, then the arc length is equal to twice the length of its radius.
