## Core idea

Graphing the modulus of a quadratic function involves taking its parabolic curve and reflecting any parts that sink below the x-axis vertically upwards. This transformation converts the standard smooth U or inverted-U curve into a continuous graph with sharp turning corners or distinct structural humps along the horizontal axis.

## Key formulas

$$
y = |ax^2 + bx + c|

\text{Roots where } ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \text{ act as the reflection boundaries.}
$$


## Steps / method

Find the roots of the quadratic equation by setting $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ to identify x-intercepts.

Find the standard vertex coordinates using completing the square or differentiation.

Sketch the base quadratic graph lightly, tracking whether it is a standard minimum or maximum shape.

Reflect any segment of the curve located below the horizontal x-axis upwards. For example, a minimum vertex at $(h, -k)$ reflects directly up to become a local maximum at $(h, k)$.

### Key rule

Reflect any part of the parabola below the $x$-axis upward; the roots of $f(x) = 0$ are the cusp points where reflection occurs.

## Worked example — Parabola with two roots

Sketch $y = |x^2 - 4x + 3|$.

Factorise: $x^2 - 4x + 3 = (x - 1)(x - 3)$, so roots are $x = 1$ and $x = 3$.

The base parabola has minimum $(2, -1)$ below the axis. Reflect this loop upward to create a local maximum at $(2, 1)$ with cusps at $(1, 0)$ and $(3, 0)$.

## Worked example — No real roots

Sketch $y = |x^2 + 2x + 5|$.

The discriminant is $4 - 20 = -16 < 0$, so the parabola never crosses the $x$-axis.

Since $f(x) > 0$ for all real $x$, the graph of $y = |f(x)|$ is identical to $y = x^2 + 2x + 5$.

## Examiner tip

Be precise when drawing the reflected components. Ensure that the corners at the x-intercepts meet sharply and do not look like smooth curves or rounded turning points, as examiners check for clear "cusps" here.

## Quick check

If a quadratic function has no real roots, then the graph of its modulus $y = |f(x)|$ looks identical to the original graph.
