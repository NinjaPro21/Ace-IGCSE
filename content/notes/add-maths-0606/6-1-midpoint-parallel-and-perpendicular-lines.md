## Core idea

Coordinate geometry uses algebraic formulas to find properties of line segments. Parallel lines share the same gradient; perpendicular lines meet at $90°$ and their gradients multiply to $-1$.

## Key formulas

**Midpoint**

$$
M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)
$$

**Gradient**

$$
m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}
$$

**Parallel and perpendicular**

$$
\text{Parallel: } m_1 = m_2
$$

$$
\text{Perpendicular: } m_1 \times m_2 = -1
$$

## Steps / method

1. **Find the gradient** using $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.

2. **Determine the relationship** — parallel lines share $m$; perpendicular lines use $m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1}$.

3. **Find the midpoint** by averaging the $x$-coordinates and $y$-coordinates separately.

### Key rule

Subtract coordinates in the **same order** for both numerator and denominator: $\frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.

## Worked example — Midpoint and perpendicular gradient

Question: Points $A(1, 2)$ and $B(5, 10)$ form a line segment. Find the midpoint $M$ and the gradient of a line perpendicular to $AB$.

Midpoint:

$$
M = \left(\frac{1 + 5}{2},\; \frac{2 + 10}{2}\right) = (3, 6)
$$

Gradient of $AB$:

$$
m = \frac{10 - 2}{5 - 1} = \frac{8}{4} = 2
$$

Perpendicular gradient: $m_\perp = -\frac{1}{2}$

## Examiner tip

When calculating gradient, always subtract in the same order (e.g. $y_2 - y_1$ over $x_2 - x_1$). Mixing the order gives the wrong sign.

## Quick check

If one line has gradient $\frac{3}{4}$ and another has gradient $-\frac{4}{3}$, they are perpendicular.
