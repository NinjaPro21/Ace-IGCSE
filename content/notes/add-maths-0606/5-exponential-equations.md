## Core idea

Exponential equations are mathematical statements where the unknown variable is located inside the exponent of a base term. Use logarithms to solve these equations by bringing the variable down from the exponent, making it accessible for standard algebraic isolation. This technique is useful for modeling population growth dynamics, radioactive decay rates, or compound financial intervals.

## Steps / method

Isolate the base term containing the exponent variable on one side of your equation before introducing logarithms.

Take the logarithm (base 10 or base e) of both sides of the equation to maintain balance.

Apply the Power Law of logarithms to move the exponent variable down in front of the log term as a linear multiplier.

Rearrange the resulting linear equation using division to isolate the variable, or use a substitution variable (u=ax) if the equation matches a hidden quadratic pattern.

## Examiner tip

When solving exponential equations that simplify to a quadratic format (e.g., equations involving 32x and 3x), remember that substituting u=3x might yield a negative value for u. You must reject any negative value of u because an exponential term like 3x can never output a negative number.

## Quick check

If you solve an exponential equation and get a step where 2x=−5, then you must state that this branch has no real solution.
