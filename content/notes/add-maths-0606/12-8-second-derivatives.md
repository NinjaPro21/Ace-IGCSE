## Core idea

The **second derivative** is the derivative of the first derivative. It measures how the gradient itself is changing and is used to describe curvature and classify stationary points.

## Key formulas

**Second derivative**

$$
\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)
$$

## Steps / method

1. **Differentiate once** to find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.
2. **Differentiate again** to find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.
3. **Apply** product, quotient, or chain rules again if the first derivative is a compound function.

### Key rule

$\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ is not the same as $\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2$ — one is the second derivative, the other is the gradient squared.

## Worked example — Polynomial

Question: Given $y = x^4 - 3x^2 + 1$, find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 4x^3 - 6x$

$\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2 - 6$

## Worked example — Using notation

Question: If $y = x^3$, find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ and $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2$ and $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x$

At $x = 2$: gradient $= 12$, curvature measure $= 12$.

## Examiner tip

Do not confuse $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ with $\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2$. They are completely different quantities.

## Quick check

For $y = x^2$, $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2x$ and $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2$.
