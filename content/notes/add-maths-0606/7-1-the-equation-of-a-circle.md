## Core idea

The equation of a circle describes all points $(x, y)$ a fixed distance (the radius) from a fixed centre. It is an application of Pythagoras' theorem on the coordinate plane.

## Key formulas

**Standard form**

$$
(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2
$$

Centre $(h, k)$, radius $r$.

**General form**

$$
x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0
$$

Centre $(-g, -f)$, radius $r = \sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c}$.

## Steps / method

1. **Identify** centre and radius if given directly — substitute into standard form.

2. **Or complete the square** — group $x$-terms and $y$-terms separately in general form.

3. **Read off** centre $(h, k)$ and radius $r$ from standard form.

### Key rule

If the equation ends with $= 49$, the radius is $7$, not $49$.

## Worked example — Finding the equation

Question: Find the equation of the circle with centre $(-3, 4)$ passing through $(1, 7)$.

$$
r^2 = (1 - (-3))^2 + (7 - 4)^2 = 16 + 9 = 25
$$

$$
(x + 3)^2 + (y - 4)^2 = 25
$$

## Worked example — General to standard form

Question: Find the centre and radius of $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 8y - 11 = 0$.

Complete the square:

$$
(x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 8y) = 11
$$

$$
(x - 3)^2 - 9 + (y + 4)^2 - 16 = 11
$$

$$
(x - 3)^2 + (y + 4)^2 = 36
$$

Centre $(3, -4)$, radius $6$.

## Examiner tip

Students often forget to square the radius when writing the equation, or forget to take the square root when reading the radius from $r^2$. Always double-check.

## Quick check

If the equation is $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, the centre is at the origin $(0, 0)$.
